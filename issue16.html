EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 16

The Thokoza-Katlehong Garment Belt

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Thokoza-Katlehong Garment Belt is not a story about sewing. It is a story about manufacturing intelligence — about how an informal cluster of cut-make-trim workshops, fabric traders, and garment finishers has built, in the southern corridor of Ekurhuleni's township economy, a production capability that formal manufacturers have been unable to replicate at comparable cost.

The deep story: how the block-by-block accumulation of sewing machines, overlockers, and cutting tables across Thokoza and Katlehong's residential-industrial fringe has produced something the formal garment sector has never managed: a flexible, relationship-driven production system that turns fabric into finished garments faster, cheaper, and with more contextual intelligence than any factory model can match — supplying church networks spanning three provinces, school uniform contracts for hundreds of institutions, and a new generation of township fashion labels that operate entirely through social media.

Three supporting micro-stories: the church uniform manufacturers of Thokoza who hold informal production relationships with Zionist Christian Church and Apostolic networks across Gauteng, Mpumalanga, and Limpopo; the fabric trading corridor along Khumalo Street where Chinese importers, local traders, and cut-make-trim operators have constructed a just-in-time supply chain invisible to any textile industry analyst; and the WhatsApp-era fashion designers of Vosloorus who design, manufacture, and distribute complete collections without a retail address, a registered company, or a single formal supply chain relationship.

The overlooked place: the Khumalo Street fabric strip in Thokoza — a three-hundred-metre corridor of fabric traders and haberdashery suppliers that functions as the raw material market for the entire informal garment cluster, and that appears on no commercial directory.

The interesting statistic: South Africa's formal clothing and textile sector employs approximately 75,000 workers in registered establishments. Researchers who have attempted sample-based estimates suggest the informal township garment sector may employ a comparable, entirely uncounted number at cost structures the formal industry cannot approach.

Weekly Issue — 8 May 2026

The Thread Economy

How the invisible garment belt of Thokoza and Katlehong became southern Africa's most productive unregistered clothing manufacturer — and why every church uniform and WhatsApp fashion label passing through the East Rand probably started life in a workshop you've never seen.

1. Editorial
What the Machines Sound Like

There is a particular sound that Thokoza's residential streets emit in the middle of the morning, in the blocks between the main arterials and the industrial fringe. It is not loud. It is not industrial in the way a factory is industrial. It is, if you listen carefully, rhythmic: the sustained percussion of industrial sewing machines running at production pace, the intermittent whirr of an overlocker finishing a seam, the brief pause when thread runs out and the resumption when it is replaced. These sounds come from behind ordinary doors in ordinary houses in streets that look, from the pavement, exactly like streets in any other part of the East Rand. The clue, if you are paying attention, is the fabric. A bolt of deep green polyester leaning against a wall. Offcuts in a refuse bag by the gate. Thread cones in colours that have no domestic purpose. These are the traces of a manufacturing operation that the formal economy has never counted and most passing observers have never thought to name.

I have been thinking about the garment cluster of the southern East Rand for some months now — not with the sentimentality that sometimes attaches to stories about township enterprise, but with the precise curiosity I would bring to any manufacturing system that is performing a function at cost and speed that the formal sector cannot match. Because that is what this is: a manufacturing system. Not a cottage industry in the diminishing sense of the phrase. A geographically concentrated, technically specialised, commercially sophisticated production network that supplies clothing to markets ranging from the Zionist Christian Church networks of Limpopo to the fashion-conscious eighteen-year-olds of Vosloorus who order what they want on WhatsApp and receive it within forty-eight hours.

The formal clothing and textile industry in South Africa has been in structural decline for decades, squeezed between rising wage costs, unreliable electricity, competition from cheap imported garments, and the inability to match the price points that the majority of South African consumers actually require. The industry's formal response has been to retreat: fewer factories, smaller workforces, more importation. While this retreat has been underway, a different kind of manufacturing has been advancing — quietly, without announcement, without subsidy, and without anyone in the formal industry's analytical framework having thought to model it. The Thokoza-Katlehong garment belt is not the replacement for the industry that contracted. It is the industry that grew in its place.

This issue is an attempt to map it — to explain what the cluster actually produces, how it is supplied, who its customers are, and why its cost structure represents something that formal industrial policy has spent decades trying to engineer through incentive schemes and industrial parks, without ever quite achieving.

2. The Long Essay
The Cut, the Make, and the Trim
Section I — The Observation

Walk the Khumalo Street corridor in Thokoza on a Wednesday morning and the garment economy announces itself through material rather than signage. The fabric traders occupy small premises and large ones, their stock compressed into every available vertical space: bolts of polyester and cotton drill stacked floor to ceiling, rolls of ceremonial fabric in the specific shades that church uniform protocols demand — a deep royal blue for one denomination, a white trimmed with gold for another, a dark green whose precise shade is known by sight by every trader on the strip. Between the fabric stalls, the haberdashery suppliers: buttons, zip fasteners, shoulder pads, iron-on interfacing, thread in fifty colours, elastic in six widths. This is not a market for domestic sewers making curtains. The quantities are commercial. The specifications are industrial. The buyers arriving by bakkie and returning with their loads are running production operations, not hobbies.

The cluster that Khumalo Street supplies has no formal boundaries. It extends through the residential streets south and east of the strip, workshop by workshop, into the adjacent fringe of Katlehong. Some operations are large enough to occupy a dedicated outbuilding or a modest industrial unit. Many are contained within the main house — the machines occupying a room partially stripped of domestic furniture and restocked with the vocabulary of garment production: cutting tables made from composite board, pattern storage hung from wire frames, the ordered complexity of a production run in progress. What appears domestic is, on inspection, commercial. What looks like a spare room is, by any industrial classification, a factory floor.

The formal clothing industry retreated. The machines moved to back rooms. The machinists became owners. The industry did not disappear. It decentralised — and in decentralising, it found a cost structure that the factory model never achieved.
Section II — The Hidden System

The garment manufacturing cluster of Thokoza and Katlehong operates through three distinct production streams, each with its own supply chain, client base, and economic logic, and each invisible to the formal textile and clothing industry's data systems.

The first and largest stream is institutional uniform manufacturing. Church uniforms are, in the East Rand's informal garment economy, what cold-chain logistics is to OR Tambo: the anchor demand that organises everything else. The Zionist Christian Church, the largest church in sub-Saharan Africa by membership, holds its major annual gathering at Moria in Limpopo each Easter. For the weeks preceding Moria, the demand for ZCC uniforms — the green and khaki combinations, the star badges, the specific accessory items that distinguish office-holders from general members — drives a production surge across the Thokoza cluster that no formal manufacturer has ever managed to meet at the required price. The informal CMT workshops have been meeting it for decades. They know the specifications by heart. They hold no formal contract with the church. Their position in the supply chain is maintained by the relationships they have built with regional church coordinators over years of reliable delivery. This is the same commercial logic that governs the Alberton South chemical formulators: specific knowledge, embedded relationship, no formal documentation, indispensable result.

The second stream is school and workwear. The East Rand's informal CMT sector supplies school uniforms to hundreds of township schools under arrangements that bypass both the formal uniform retailers and the school fee procurement system. A school principal approaches a known Thokoza operator, agrees a price per garment for the required quantities in the required colours, and collects the finished goods within a week. The price is below anything the formal uniform industry can offer. The turnaround beats any catalogue supplier. The operator knows the exact cutting pattern for every standard school uniform configuration in the East Rand because she has cut each of them, repeatedly, across many years. Her pattern library is not digital. It is physical: cardboard templates worn at the edges from use, annotated in pencil with fit adjustments learned from the specific measurements of East Rand school-age children rather than from any standard sizing table. That annotation is institutional knowledge. It was not taught. It was accumulated.

The third stream is the newest and, in terms of growth trajectory, the most significant: social media fashion production. Across Vosloorus and southern Katlehong, a generation of designers operating under labels with Instagram followings in the tens of thousands are producing seasonal collections — tracksuits, streetwear, occasion wear — that they design digitally and manufacture through the informal CMT network. The designer creates the pattern or briefs a pattern-cutter in the cluster. The fabric is sourced from the Khumalo Street traders. The CMT workshop produces the run — anywhere from twenty to two hundred units. The finished product is photographed, posted, and sold through WhatsApp before it reaches any physical retail environment. The logistics are handled by taxi freight and courier networks. The payment is mobile money. The entire supply chain, from design brief to customer delivery, operates without a single formal commercial institution. It is, in the language of venture capital, a vertically integrated manufacturing and retail operation. Its founders call it hustle.

Section III — The Broader Insight

What the Thokoza-Katlehong garment cluster reveals about post-industrial Ekurhuleni is a principle that this publication has traced in several other sectors, but which takes on particular clarity in the garment context: informal manufacturing clusters are not informal because they lack the capability to be formal. They are informal because formality, at the cost and scale at which they operate, would make them uneconomic. The church uniform manufacturer who operates from her converted back room has overheads that no formal factory can approach. She pays no commercial rent. She has no HR department, no compliance officer, no procurement manager. The sewing machines she uses were purchased second-hand and maintained by the same technically inclined neighbour who maintains every other machine in the cluster, for a service fee paid informally and recouped through a client referral network. Her cost per garment is determined by fabric price, thread price, and the value she places on her own labour time — and she determines that last figure herself, which means she can absorb a rush order by working through the weekend in a way that no formal factory shift system could accommodate.

This flexibility is not a workaround. It is the competitive advantage. The client who needs five hundred church uniforms in nine days does not call a formal manufacturer, because formal manufacturers cannot guarantee that timeline at that price. She calls the Thokoza cluster, because the cluster can and does. The capability that produces this outcome is not informal in the sense of being improvised or uncertain. It is informal in the sense of being organised outside the institutional framework that formal analysis uses to assess manufacturing capacity. The output is entirely real. The garments are worn. The churches are uniformed. The schools are clothed. The Instagram followers receive their orders. The economy, in this specific dimension, works — just not in a way that any industry survey has yet thought to measure.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The garment cluster of Thokoza and Katlehong has exhibited, across the decade of formal textile industry decline that has run from roughly 2015 to the present, the property that should by now be recognisable to readers of this publication: it grew as the formal sector contracted. Each formal clothing factory that closed in the East Rand's industrial belt released sewing machinists into the labour market. Some found formal employment elsewhere. Many did not. Those who did not took their machines — sometimes literally, purchasing equipment at the close-out valuations of factories being wound up — and established the bedroom workshops and backyard CMT operations that now constitute the cluster's productive core. The capital that the formal sector discarded became the founding asset of the informal sector's expansion.

The same dynamic operated during the load-shedding peak. Formal garment manufacturers, dependent on consistent three-phase power for their industrial cutting and sewing equipment, faced production losses during extended outage windows. The cluster's smaller workshops, whose individual machine loads were manageable on inverter backup power, maintained production continuity. Clients who had previously used formal manufacturers began, out of necessity, testing the informal cluster. Many did not return when the power stabilised. The crisis had, as it tends to in this region, revealed the alternative that the formal sector's pricing had made invisible. The thread economy is stronger now than it was before the crises that were supposed to damage it. The pattern holds. It has held across every sector this publication has examined. An economy built at street level, organised around relationships rather than contracts, and calibrated to the actual purchasing power of the people it serves is an economy that benefits from the disruptions that damage the institutions above it. The machines in the back rooms of Thokoza are not a fallback. They are a foundation.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Church Uniform Coordinators
Thokoza Residential Streets

In the weeks before major church gatherings — particularly the annual ZCC pilgrimage to Moria and the Easter conventions of the Apostolic denominations — a specific category of operator becomes central to the Thokoza cluster's logistics: the church uniform coordinator. These individuals hold informal relationships with both the church networks they supply and the CMT workshops that produce the garments. They receive orders from regional church coordinators across Gauteng, Mpumalanga, and Limpopo — aggregating individual member requirements into production runs — and distribute them to the workshops with the capacity to deliver on time. They charge a coordination margin on the retail price, manage quality checking of completed garments, and handle the distribution logistics that move finished uniforms from Thokoza to church depots hundreds of kilometres away. They are, in the precise functional sense, an informal supply chain management layer operating a logistics business that no formal intermediary has entered because the margins at individual-order scale are too thin and the relationship knowledge required is too deep. No one has given them a job title. Their function is indispensable regardless.

2. The Pattern Keepers
Katlehong Southern Fringe

Within the garment cluster, a small number of operators have built their competitive position around a single capability: pattern drafting and grading. These are the individuals who translate a designer's sketch, a client's reference garment, or a church denomination's specification into the precise cardboard templates that guide every cut of fabric in the production run. Their services are in constant demand because patterns are the cluster's intellectual property — the operational knowledge that allows a CMT workshop to reproduce a uniform to specification without ever seeing the original. The pattern keepers charge per pattern, per size grade, and per modification. Several hold physical libraries covering hundreds of garment specifications accumulated across decades: annotated cardboard templates in stacked flat files, worn at the edges from use, marked with fit adjustments learned from the specific size distributions of East Rand bodies rather than from any standard sizing table. These libraries are the cluster's most concentrated store of knowledge. They are also entirely unreplicated: no digital pattern service has yet built the local calibration that makes them useful here.

3. The Social Media Labels
Vosloorus Extended

Along the residential streets of Vosloorus Extended, a cluster of micro fashion labels operates through Instagram and WhatsApp with commercial sophistication that belies their informal structure. The typical operation: one designer who handles concept and client communication, one or two CMT partners in the broader Thokoza-Katlehong cluster who handle cutting and sewing, a network of part-time photographers who produce the content, and a logistics relationship with a local courier or taxi freight operator who handles delivery. The labels carry no registered brand, no fixed address, and no formal retail relationship. Several have follower counts in the five-figure range and sell out production runs of two hundred units within forty-eight hours of posting. Revenue flows through mobile money. The operations are, by any reasonable measure, small manufacturing businesses running fully integrated design-to-delivery supply chains. They simply do not appear in any manufacturing data. The gap between what they are and how they are classified is not a regulatory anomaly. It is a measurement failure — and a persistent one.

4. The Map of the Week
The Khumalo Street Fabric Strip, Thokoza
26°22′S  28°6′E

Approximately three hundred metres. That is the active length of the Khumalo Street fabric trading corridor in Thokoza — a strip where the raw material supply chain of the East Rand's informal garment cluster is most visibly concentrated. In those three hundred metres you will find: the large fabric traders whose stock includes the ceremonial materials demanded by the region's major church networks; the smaller haberdashery operators stocked to the ceiling with the accessories and finishing materials that production runs require; and, at the corridor's edges, the first-stage CMT operators who buy fabric in the morning and return completed cut panels to their workshop partners by afternoon. The street has no industrial zoning classification. It has no commercial district designation. It is, officially, a residential street with commercial activity. What it is, functionally, is a textile exchange — the supply heart of a manufacturing cluster that clothes a significant fraction of the East Rand's population and a significant portion of its spiritual life.

To stand on Khumalo Street on a Thursday morning, when the church uniform season is approaching its peak and fabric traders are taking orders for the weekend's production runs, is to understand that Ekurhuleni makes things. It simply does not always make them in the places where manufacturing is supposed to happen. The factory is in the back room. The exchange is on the pavement. The supply chain is in a WhatsApp group. The economy is real regardless.

5. The East Rand Number
~75,000 Registered workers in South Africa's formal clothing and textile sector — with informal township garment manufacturing estimated by researchers to employ a comparable, uncounted number at cost structures the formal industry cannot match

South Africa's formal clothing, textile, footwear, and leather sector employs approximately 75,000 workers in registered establishments — one of the country's most significant formal manufacturing employer categories. What no official statistic captures is the parallel labour market in the informal garment sector: the CMT workshops, the pattern-cutters, the church uniform coordinators, the social media label operators, the Khumalo Street fabric traders — concentrated in the township clusters of Gauteng's industrial fringe and replicated, at varying scales, across the country's major metropolitan areas. Researchers who have attempted sample-based estimates of informal garment employment consistently suggest the sector's true scale may rival the formal sector's headcount. The garments are just as real. The employment is just as real. The statistic that accurately describes either does not yet exist.

6. Closing Reflection
What the Machines Leave Behind

There is a version of industrial decline that looks, from the outside, like a story of pure loss: factories closed, employment gone, a sector diminished. The Thokoza-Katlehong garment belt tells a different version of the same history. The factories closed. The machines moved to back rooms. The machinists became owners. The production continued — at different addresses, at different scales, for different clients, under different terms. The industry did not disappear. It decentralised. And in decentralising, it found a flexibility, a responsiveness to local demand, and a cost structure that the formal model had never achieved and cannot now replicate. The thread that the formal sector dropped was picked up by someone else. In Thokoza, that thread is still running — through every church uniform produced for a congregation the formal industry never thought to serve, every school blazer cut to the exact proportions of the children who will wear it, every WhatsApp order dispatched on the day it was placed. The economy is in the seams.
