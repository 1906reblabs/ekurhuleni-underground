## THEME ANALYSIS — ISSUE 24

The theme of the Decant Economy is not a story about pollution. It is a story about a debt the East Rand inherited from a century of gold mining — an underground void a billion cubic metres wide, filling with acidic water year on year — and about the informal watchers, brokers, and adapters who have built a livelihood around managing what the state's remediation infrastructure was never quite built to finish.

**The deep story:** how the ongoing management of acid mine drainage across Ekurhuleni's Central and Eastern mining basins — water pumped from flooded voids beneath Boksburg, Germiston, and Springs, treated at state plants, and discharged into the Blesbokspruit and the Vaal system — has generated a shadow economy of its own: informal water-level watchers who track decant risk before it is officially announced, brokers who move treatment by-products into local markets, and wetland communities who have quietly rebuilt their livelihoods around water that is not what it once was.

**Three supporting micro-stories:** the informal level-watchers around Boksburg Lake and the old ERPM shaft complex, whose daily reading of water colour and clarity has, on at least one recent occasion, outpaced official confirmation of a decant event; the gypsum and lime sludge brokers working the fringes of the Eastern Basin treatment plant at Grootvlei, who move the by-products of neutralisation into small-scale plaster and soil-conditioning markets the formal chemical sector does not reach; and the smallholders and reed-harvesters of the Blesbokspruit wetland corridor, who have adapted planting cycles and harvesting zones around a water chemistry that shifts with the treatment plants' fortunes.

**The overlooked place:** the Blesbokspruit wetland corridor between Springs and Nigel, downstream of the Grootvlei Mine Shaft 3 treatment works — a Ramsar-listed wetland whose water quality has become a quiet barometer of the state's remediation performance.

**The interesting statistic:** Ekurhuleni's Central and Eastern Basin treatment plants were built with a combined design capacity to treat up to roughly 190 million litres of acidic mine water daily — a volume that, when the pumps fail, becomes a countdown.

### 1. Editorial

**What the Water Remembers**

There is a specific shade of orange that the water in Boksburg Lake is not supposed to be. Locals who fish its margins, or simply walk the path that rings it most mornings, know the ordinary palette of the lake by heart — the green-brown of algae in late summer, the flat grey of a still winter morning. In mid-2025, the lake turned a colour that had nothing to do with algae or weather: a rusty, mineral red that spread from an inflow point over a matter of days. The municipality had not yet commented. The state agency responsible for the region's acid mine drainage infrastructure had not yet confirmed a cause. The people who walk the lake every morning had already worked it out.

I have been curious for some time about what happens in the interval between an environmental event occurring underground, in a flooded mining void nobody can see, and that event being formally announced above ground. It is not a small interval. Water levels in the Witwatersrand's old gold-mining basins are monitored, but the monitoring is instrumented, agency-run, and reported on a schedule that does not always match the schedule on which acid mine water actually reaches the surface. Into that gap — between instrumentation and announcement — a set of informal actors has moved: people who read colour, smell, and fish behaviour as evidence, and who have, on more than one occasion, understood that something was wrong before any statement said so.

This is only the most visible layer of a larger economy that has grown up around Ekurhuleni's acid mine drainage problem. Beneath the level-watching, a market in treatment by-products — the gypsum and lime sludge that the state's neutralisation plants produce in bulk — moves quietly into local construction and agricultural use. Further downstream, along the Blesbokspruit wetland that receives the treated, and periodically the undertreated, water, smallholders and reed-harvesters have spent a decade adjusting their livelihoods to a water chemistry that shifts with the treatment plants' funding and maintenance cycles.

None of this is illegal, dramatic, or particularly visible from the road. It is also not new: the acid mine drainage problem beneath the East Rand has been managed, imperfectly, for over a decade, and the economy that has formed around that management has had exactly as long to mature. This issue is an attempt to map it — the watchers, the brokers, and the adapters who have built a working relationship with a problem the state has never quite finished solving.

### 2. The Long Essay

**The Void Beneath the City**

**Section I — The Observation**

Drive the service road that skirts the Eastern Basin treatment plant at Grootvlei Mine Shaft 3, on the northern edge of Springs, and the infrastructure of acid mine drainage management presents itself with unusual plainness: settling ponds the colour of weak tea, a chemical dosing building, a flare of white gypsum precipitate drying at the edge of a containment cell. The plant exists to do one job — hold the water level in the flooded mining void below the environmental critical level, the depth beneath which acid water begins to threaten the dolomite aquifers and drinking-water sources of the wider region — and it does that job by pumping, dosing, and discharging, continuously, into the Blesbokspruit. When the pumps work, the system is close to invisible: treated water leaves the plant clean enough to sustain a Ramsar-listed wetland a few kilometres downstream. When the pumps fail — as they periodically have, through mechanical breakdown, funding gaps, or contractual disputes — the water level beneath the city begins, quietly, to rise.

**Section II — The Hidden System**

The hidden economy around Ekurhuleni's acid mine drainage infrastructure operates across three registers, each responding to a different part of the treatment cycle.

The first is informal water-level intelligence. The state's monitoring of the Central and Eastern Basin voids is instrumented and reported through the Trans-Caledon Tunnel Authority and the Department of Water and Sanitation, but the reporting cadence is administrative rather than continuous, and the underlying estimates of exactly how many metres below surface a decant will occur are, by the agencies' own account, imprecise. Into that uncertainty, a layer of local observation has developed: anglers and plot-holders around Boksburg Lake, workers and residents near the old ERPM shaft complex, who have learned to read colour change, smell, and dead or distressed fish as leading indicators of a decant, sometimes days ahead of official confirmation. Their knowledge is not systematic in the way a monitoring borehole is systematic. It is observational, embodied, and — on the evidence of the 2025 Boksburg Lake decant, where community reports of discolouration preceded the municipality's public acknowledgement — frequently faster.

The second register is the treatment by-product economy. The high-density sludge process used at the Witwatersrand basin plants produces, as a deliberate output rather than a waste stream, significant volumes of gypsum and metal hydroxide sludge — material that international research has repeatedly identified as commercially recoverable for plasterboard, soil conditioning, and construction use, though South Africa's formal beneficiation of it remains limited relative to the volumes produced. Around the treatment plants, small operators have moved into the gap between what the formal by-product market could use and what it currently does: brokering surplus gypsum and lime material into local plaster suppliers and small agricultural users at a fraction of commercial gypsum's price, in transactions that leave no trace in the treatment plants' own production statistics.

The third register is downstream livelihood adaptation. The Blesbokspruit — a former Ramsar Wetland of International Importance now listed on the Montreux Record because of the water-quality pressure it has experienced — supports smallholders, reed-harvesters, and small-scale irrigators whose practices have shifted, plot by plot, around a water chemistry that is not constant. Harvesting zones move. Planting cycles adjust to periods of higher sulphate and conductivity. None of this adaptation is coordinated by any agency. It has simply happened, incrementally, over the decade the treatment plants have been operating.

> *The gap between when the water rises and when the agency says so is administrative, not physical — and nobody beside the shaft needs permission to notice.*

**Section III — The Broader Insight**

What the decant economy reveals about Ekurhuleni is a pattern this publication has traced before in different registers: informal knowledge systems fill the gap left by formal monitoring precisely where the formal system's reporting cadence lags the pace of the underlying physical event. The state's instrumentation is not wrong, exactly — it produces water-level data of real precision. But the interval between measurement and public disclosure is an administrative interval, not a physical one, and the people who live and work closest to the affected water have every incentive to close that gap themselves.

This matters for how Ekurhuleni thinks about environmental risk communication. The standard model treats residents as recipients of official information: the agency measures, the agency discloses, the public responds. The acid mine drainage story of the East Rand suggests a different and additional model is already operating in practice — residents as an unpaid, uncredentialed, and frequently accurate early-warning layer, whose observations precede and sometimes contradict the official timeline. A city that formally integrated this observational capacity, rather than treating it as anecdote to be confirmed or dismissed, would have a faster and cheaper monitoring network than any additional borehole instrumentation could provide.

**Section IV — The Antifragile Angle**

The informal layers of the decant economy have a specific relationship to the state system's failures: they expand precisely when the formal infrastructure is under the most strain. When the Eastern Basin plant's pumps failed through 2021 and 2022 — a period in which all three deep-level abstraction pumps at the facility were simultaneously out of service for a stretch of months — the level-watching economy around the affected shafts intensified, because the gap between what residents needed to know and what the agency was disclosing had widened. The same dynamic applies to the by-product economy: treatment-plant funding and maintenance disputes that slow formal beneficiation projects do not reduce the volume of gypsum sludge produced. They leave more of it, for longer, sitting in containment cells at the plant boundary — exactly the condition under which informal brokers find it easiest to negotiate access.

This is not a case of the informal system celebrating dysfunction. It is a case of the informal system being structurally positioned to absorb the consequences of dysfunction faster than the formal system can respond to it. Each maintenance failure, each funding gap, each missed environmental critical level target widens the space in which local observation, by-product brokerage, and downstream livelihood adjustment do the work that the state's own remediation programme has not yet finished doing.

### 3. The Hidden Economy

**Three Operations Nobody Is Watching**

**1. The Level Watchers**
BOKSBURG LAKE / OLD ERPM SHAFT COMPLEX, CENTRAL BASIN

Around Boksburg Lake and the decommissioned ERPM shaft precincts nearby, a loose network of anglers, dog-walkers, and plot-holders has developed an informal early-warning practice: daily observation of water colour, clarity, and fish behaviour as leading indicators of an acid mine drainage decant. Their method has no instrumentation and no institutional standing. What it has is repetition — the same people checking the same stretch of shoreline most mornings for years — and, in the 2025 Boksburg Lake discolouration, a documented case of community reports preceding official confirmation. They are not paid for this. They pass what they see to each other, to local anglers' associations, and to community messaging groups that move faster than any agency statement.

**2. The Sludge Brokers**
GROOTVLEI / EASTERN BASIN TREATMENT PLANT FRINGE, SPRINGS

On the access roads bordering the Eastern Basin plant at Grootvlei Mine Shaft 3, small operators broker surplus gypsum and lime sludge — the deliberate by-product of the high-density sludge neutralisation process — into local plaster suppliers, soil-conditioning users, and small construction operations. International research has repeatedly identified this material as commercially recoverable; South Africa's formal beneficiation of it lags the volumes produced. The brokers who move it locally charge a fraction of commercial gypsum's price, negotiate access informally with plant contractors, and rely on relationships built over years rather than any supply agreement. Their business exists in the space between what the treatment process discards as waste and what the formal by-product market has not yet organised itself to capture.

**3. The Wetland Adapters**
BLESBOKSPRUIT CORRIDOR, SPRINGS–NIGEL BOUNDARY

Along the Blesbokspruit — a former Ramsar Wetland of International Importance now listed on the Montreux Record because of sustained water-quality pressure — smallholders and reed-harvesters have spent a decade adjusting planting cycles, irrigation timing, and harvesting zones around a water chemistry that shifts with the fortunes of the treatment plants upstream. When sulphate and conductivity rise during a maintenance failure, harvesting moves to less-affected reaches; when the plants recover, cultivation follows the water back. None of this adaptation is coordinated by any conservation authority. It is knowledge accumulated by people whose livelihood depends on reading a wetland that a state remediation programme, however well-funded, has never fully stabilised.

### 4. The Map of the Week

**The Blesbokspruit Wetland Corridor, Springs–Nigel**

*26°19′S  28°26′E*

Roughly twelve kilometres. That is the working length of the Blesbokspruit wetland corridor as it runs south from Springs toward Nigel, receiving treated water from the Eastern Basin acid mine drainage plant at Grootvlei Mine Shaft 3 before continuing toward the Marievale bird sanctuary and the Vaal River system beyond. In that corridor you will find: reed beds harvested on a rotation calibrated to water quality rather than season; smallholder plots irrigated from channels whose reliability depends on a treatment plant's maintenance schedule; and, closer to the plant itself, the informal gypsum trade that moves the neutralisation process's by-product into local hands before it is counted as anything at all.

What you will not find is any signage explaining the relationship between the wetland's health and the pumps several kilometres upstream. The connection is legible only to the people who have watched both change together, year on year. To walk this corridor after a plant outage — when the water carries more sulphate than usual and the reed harvesters have moved their cutting further downstream — is to understand that Ekurhuleni's mining legacy has not ended. It has simply moved from underground to the surface, in instalments, exactly as fast as a pump can fail.

### 5. The East Rand Number

**~190 Mℓ/day**

**Combined design capacity of the Central and Eastern Basin acid mine drainage treatment plants — the volume Ekurhuleni's remediation infrastructure was built to process daily to keep the flooded mining void below its environmental critical level**

The Central Basin plant at the old ERPM shaft was designed to treat approximately 82 million litres of acidic mine water daily; the Eastern Basin plant at Grootvlei was designed for up to 110 million litres, though its documented operational range has more often sat between 70 and 100 million litres. Together, the two plants represent Ekurhuleni's primary defence against an underground void that, left unpumped, would decant within months. The number is large. It has also, on more than one occasion since 2016, been reduced to zero by a failed pump motor.

---

### 6. Closing Reflection

**What the Lake Already Knew**

By the time Ekurhuleni's environmental officials confirmed, in mid-2025, that acid mine drainage had turned Boksburg Lake red, the residents who fish and walk its margins had already spent several days describing precisely that. Their account did not require a laboratory, a press office, or a state agency's sign-off. It required attention, memory of what the water usually looks like, and no particular incentive to wait for permission to say what they had seen. The acid mine drainage beneath the East Rand will not be resolved soon; the underground void is too large, the treatment costs too permanent. The question worth asking is smaller and more answerable: whose observations does the city choose to count as evidence, and whose does it wait to confirm.
