EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 6

The Parallel Grid

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Parallel Grid is not a story about power cuts. It is a story about what happens when an industrial economy refuses to stop — when the infrastructure it depends on fails consistently enough that the economy builds a replacement, quietly and at its own expense, until the replacement becomes more reliable than the original.

The deep story: how the years of severe load-shedding that peaked across Ekurhuleni's industrial belt between 2022 and 2024 produced something that no energy policy designed: a distributed, adaptive, largely informal electricity infrastructure — rooftop solar fields, diesel generator networks, LPG switching stations, and battery inverter chains — that has permanently altered the energy geography of the East Rand and now operates independently of whether Eskom is stable or not.

Three supporting micro-stories: the generator fuel economy that grew along the Boksburg-Germiston industrial spine, where diesel dealers developed hyper-local delivery logistics that rival formal fuel retail in speed and coverage; the informal solar panel and battery installation networks operating across Vosloorus, Katlehong, and Tembisa, who built a skills base and a customer network that formal electrical contractors have struggled to compete with; and the shared generator agreements between neighbouring factory blocks in the Alrode and Isando industrial zones — informal power cooperatives that distribute load and cost across multiple businesses without a single legal instrument governing them.

The overlooked place: the Isando industrial strip — a compact belt of warehouses and light manufacturers running west of Kempton Park, where a higher-than-average concentration of solar arrays and generator infrastructure has created what is, in effect, a partially self-powered industrial zone that no grid map accurately reflects.

The interesting statistic: Ekurhuleni's industrial sector is estimated to have invested upwards of R4 billion in private backup and alternative energy infrastructure between 2021 and 2024 — investment that built not just capacity but an entirely new layer of energy service businesses, installation skills, and fuel logistics whose value to the regional economy outlasts the crisis that created them.

Weekly Issue — 27 February 2026

The Parallel Grid

How Ekurhuleni's industrial economy built its own electricity infrastructure during the load-shedding years — and why the grid it built is now more consequential than the grid it replaced.

1. Editorial
What the Darkness Made

Walk into almost any medium-sized factory in the Boksburg-Germiston industrial belt in the hours before dawn, and the first thing you notice is that the lights are on. This is not remarkable in itself. What is remarkable is why they are on. In a majority of these facilities, the lights are on not because Eskom is supplying power but because something else is. A diesel generator, perhaps — its exhaust vented through a hole in the corrugated roof, its fuel tank fed from a drum that was delivered yesterday by a man with a bakkie and a hand pump and a phone contact you got from someone at the industrial association. Or a battery inverter bank, charged during the previous night's on-peak window and now running the lighting, the refrigeration, the security system. Or, increasingly, a solar array on the roof whose output is tracked on a monitoring app and whose performance report the factory owner reads over his morning coffee with more attention than he ever paid to the Eskom bill.

Load-shedding ended. Or rather, load-shedding as a daily operational reality ended in 2024, when Eskom's generation capacity stabilised sufficiently to stop the scheduled outages that had structured life in this region for the better part of two years. The politicians noticed immediately. The economists noted the relief. What almost nobody paid attention to was what the crisis had left behind: an infrastructure. Not a metaphorical infrastructure — an actual one, made of silicon panels and lead-acid batteries and diesel tanks and copper cable and the accumulated knowledge of several thousand people who learned, under pressure, how to build and maintain distributed power systems at commercial scale.

I have been thinking about this infrastructure for some months now. What interests me is not the crisis that produced it — that story has been told, mostly as complaint — but what the infrastructure has become now that the crisis is over. The solar panels did not come down when the power came back. The generator service businesses did not close. The informal electricians who wired a thousand inverter systems across Katlehong and Vosloorus did not stop working. They expanded. The parallel grid that load-shedding forced Ekurhuleni to build has turned out to be, in several important respects, better than the grid it supplemented. And it is not going away.

This issue is an attempt to describe what the parallel grid actually looks like — where it is, who built it, who maintains it, and why it now represents one of the more consequential transformations in Ekurhuleni's industrial geography in the past decade. Not because of what it tells us about load-shedding, but because of what it tells us about the East Rand's capacity to build essential infrastructure from scratch, without a plan, under pressure, and make it last.

2. The Long Essay
The Grid That Crisis Built
Section I — The Observation

Drive along the R21 between OR Tambo and the Boksburg interchange on a clear afternoon, and look up. The rooflines tell a story the road does not. Above the warehouses and cold-storage facilities and light manufacturing plants, a scattering of photovoltaic panels has appeared in the past three years that was not there before — blue-black rectangles angled south, some in neat rows covering an entire roof, some in smaller clusters occupying a corner. From the ground these installations are easy to miss, their colour blending with corrugated steel in certain light. From the elevated freeway they are unmissable — a new layer of infrastructure installed on top of the existing one, without ceremony, without municipal announcement, driven entirely by the economic logic of keeping production running when the grid could not be relied upon.

This is the most visible expression of the parallel grid. It is not the most interesting one. The solar installations visible from the R21 are mostly the formal tier: large commercial arrays, professionally installed, grid-tied with inverter systems that seamlessly switch between Eskom power and stored solar depending on availability and cost. They are documented, insured, and financially legible. They are the part of the story that the press release version of Ekurhuleni's energy transition describes. Below them — literally below them in many cases, at the level of the loading bays, the back yards, the second-unit spaces between large facilities — a different kind of installation is more common and far less photographed.

Load-shedding did not damage Ekurhuleni's industrial economy into submission. It trained it — into a distributed energy intelligence that no single utility can now replicate.
Section II — The Hidden System

The parallel grid of the East Rand operates through three distinct but deeply intertwined systems, each having developed its own economy, its own skilled workforce, and its own supply chain over the course of the load-shedding years.

The first is the generator and diesel economy. At its peak, scheduled load-shedding in the Ekurhuleni industrial belt ran to eight hours a day across multiple stages. The immediate industrial response was the diesel generator — already present in some facilities, rapidly acquired by many more. This surge in generator demand created an equally rapid surge in associated services: the diesel delivery businesses that spread across the industrial zones of Boksburg, Germiston, and Alrode are not simply fuel suppliers. They are logistics operations calibrated to the specific load-shedding schedule of their clients' grid zones, delivering fuel in advance of outage windows, maintaining on-call top-up services for clients whose consumption spiked unpredictably, and — in several documented cases — pooling delivery routes with competing suppliers to cover more geographic ground efficiently. The fuel delivery economy that emerged was, in its operational sophistication, indistinguishable from a formal logistics business. Most of the operators who built it had never worked in logistics before. They learned by doing, under the pressure of clients who could not afford to run dry.

The second system is the solar and battery installation economy, and it is the one that has most durably reshaped the region's energy geography. The demand for rooftop solar and battery inverter systems that exploded during the load-shedding crisis created an installation labour market that formal electrical contracting could not adequately supply. Licensed electrical contractors were booked weeks ahead. Their prices reflected both demand and the compliance cost of formal certification. Into this gap moved a wave of informal and semi-formal installers — mostly young men, many from Katlehong, Vosloorus, and Thokoza, who trained themselves on the job, developed practical competence faster than any formal qualification course could produce it, and built client networks through word-of-mouth referral chains that now constitute a durable alternative installation economy. The systems they install are real. The panels are the same panels a formal contractor would use. The wiring, where it is done carefully, is sound. The monitoring apps work. The gap between formal and informal, in this market, has narrowed to price and paperwork — not to quality of outcome.

The third system is the generator cooperative economy — informal power-sharing arrangements between adjacent businesses in the same industrial block that emerged organically from the practical problem of generator sizing. A single generator large enough to power an entire medium-sized factory is expensive to purchase, expensive to fuel, and expensive to maintain. A generator sized for thirty percent of peak demand — covering lighting, security, and critical refrigeration — is considerably more affordable. Several factory owners in the same block discovered, during the first severe load-shedding waves, that they had independently reached the same sizing decision, and that their load profiles did not overlap in the way they had feared. One needed power in the early morning for the first production shift; the neighbour needed it in the late afternoon for cold storage. The generator could be shared. No contract was signed. The arrangement was governed by trust, by the evident mutual benefit, and by the social accountability of people who share a loading bay, a security guard rotation, and a decade of industrial-zone proximity. These informal cooperatives — there are several dozen documented in the Alrode, Isando, and Boksburg East industrial zones — represent a form of distributed energy management that no utility has designed and no policy has encouraged. They work because the economics are obvious and the relationships are real.

Section III — The Broader Insight

What the parallel grid reveals about Ekurhuleni's industrial economy is something that the load-shedding narrative consistently obscured: the region's capacity for rapid, self-organised infrastructure investment is considerably greater than its official infrastructure record suggests. South Africa spent the load-shedding years debating whose fault the crisis was, which political decision had caused which capacity shortfall, whose budget should pay for which repair. Ekurhuleni's industrial economy, in parallel, simply built a replacement. Not a complete replacement — the Eskom grid remains essential for baseload and for the heavy-industrial applications that no battery or solar system can yet serve economically. But a substantial, functional, distributed supplement that has reduced the region's exposure to grid failure by a margin that no study has yet quantified but that every factory owner in the Boksburg belt can feel in their monthly electricity bill.

The broader implication is for how South Africa thinks about energy infrastructure going forward. The parallel grid that Ekurhuleni built was not planned. It emerged from distributed decisions made by individual economic actors responding to a shared problem. Its installation workforce was not trained by any TVET college. Its logistics were not organised by any industry association. Its financing was not facilitated by any development bank. It was built by people who needed it, using skills they developed under pressure, funded by businesses that could not afford to wait. The resulting infrastructure is imperfect, uneven, and in some cases technically non-compliant. It is also real, functional, and durable. The policy question it raises is not how to replace it with something more formal. It is how to extend and support it without destroying the adaptive intelligence that produced it.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The parallel grid of the East Rand is, almost by definition, antifragile. It was built by stress. It grew stronger as the stress intensified. And it has retained — in fact, expanded — its capacity even as the stress that produced it has diminished. This is the paradox of infrastructure built under crisis conditions: it outlasts the crisis because it is integrated into the economic practices of the actors who built it. The factory that installed rooftop solar during load-shedding did not remove the panels when the power came back. It reconfigured them as a cost-reduction measure, shaving daytime electricity consumption during peak tariff hours. The diesel delivery business that built its routes around outage schedules did not dissolve when the schedules ended. It pivoted to serving the generator maintenance market and the fuel requirements of the remaining standby systems. The informal solar installer who wired fifty inverter systems in Vosloorus during 2023 did not stop working. He hired two assistants and began bidding on small commercial projects in the formal sector.

Each adaptation compounded the original investment. The crisis created the capability; the end of the crisis revealed how durable the capability was. This is a pattern worth understanding carefully, because it suggests that the next infrastructure crisis Ekurhuleni faces — whatever form it takes — will find a regional economy that has already learned how to build its own replacement. That learning is not stored in any policy document. It is stored in the accumulated practice of several thousand businesses and operators who discovered, through necessity, that they could build essential infrastructure without asking for permission. That discovery does not unhappen. The parallel grid is a fact now — as permanent in the economic geography of the East Rand as the Eskom pylons it supplements, and considerably younger.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Diesel Circuit Riders
Boksburg / Germiston Industrial Spine

From a base in the Germiston industrial fringe, a small fleet of diesel delivery operators runs what has become one of the most intelligently routed fuel services in the East Rand. Each driver knows the load-shedding zone schedules of every client on the route — schedules that shifted repeatedly during the peak crisis years and that the drivers tracked with greater fidelity than most grid management systems. What began as emergency fuel delivery has evolved into a managed energy supply relationship: the operators now monitor client generator run-hours remotely using simple telematics, deliver on predictive schedules rather than reactive calls, and maintain buffer stocks at key industrial nodes for same-hour emergency top-ups. Their clients — food processors, cold-storage operators, pharmaceutical manufacturers — pay a modest premium over pump price for a service that has never, in three years of operation, let a critical client run dry. They are, in all but name, energy logistics operators. They call themselves fuel guys.

2. The Inverter Crews
Vosloorus / Katlehong / Thokoza Residential and Light Commercial

A loose network of young electricians — most in their mid-twenties, most from the townships that bracket the East Rand's industrial zones — has built a steady small-commercial installation business from the skills they developed wiring household inverter systems during the peak load-shedding years. They now work on spaza shops, small service businesses, hair salons, tuck shops, and the backyard workshops that constitute the township's light manufacturing base. Their installations are practical rather than elegant: the cable routes are functional, the battery enclosures are improvised, the monitoring is done with a phone app rather than a dedicated system. But they cost sixty percent of what a formal electrical contractor would charge, they complete the job within days rather than weeks, and they carry the social warranty of the township referral network — which is to say, the warranty that matters most to their clients. The TVET college did not train these men. The load-shedding crisis did.

3. The Isando Power Cooperative
Isando Industrial Strip, Kempton Park East

In a cluster of light industrial units along the Isando strip, six businesses share two large diesel generators through an arrangement that began as a conversation over a boundary fence in early 2023 and has never been formalised beyond a handshake and a WhatsApp group. The cooperative allocates generator time by a priority schedule agreed informally each Monday morning, based on each business's production requirements for the week. Fuel costs are split proportionally by run-time, tracked on a shared spreadsheet. Maintenance is handled by a seventh business — a small diesel engine repair shop at the end of the strip — in exchange for guaranteed backup power during its own production hours. No lawyer has reviewed this arrangement. No accountant has formalised it. It has operated continuously for three years without a significant dispute. It is, in the specific sense that matters here, a model — not because it is replicable by policy, but because it demonstrates what economic actors do when they are given a real problem and genuine mutual interest. They solve it.

4. The Map of the Week
The Isando Solar Corridor, Kempton Park East
26°8′S  28°11′E

Approximately four kilometres. That is the length of the Isando industrial strip running east from its junction with the R21 toward the Kempton Park commercial fringe — a corridor of warehouses, light manufacturers, and cold-storage facilities that has, over the past three years, acquired one of the highest densities of rooftop solar installations of any industrial precinct in Ekurhuleni. From the air, the strip reads as a patchwork of blue-black panels interrupted by the older silver of unmodified corrugated roofs. On the ground, the evidence is subtler: the conduit runs on exterior walls, the inverter housings bolted beside loading bay doors, the metering panels that now carry two connection points where one existed before.

There is no sign naming this as an energy district. No municipality has designated it. No investment promotion body has claimed it. The solar corridor emerged because a concentration of cold-chain and pharmaceutical-adjacent businesses — industries whose operating tolerance for power interruption is near zero — happened to cluster here, and because the businesses that served their energy needs followed them in. To walk the Isando strip today is to understand that Ekurhuleni's energy geography has already changed. The map that Eskom draws of its network does not show this. The map that matters is the one on the rooftops.

5. The East Rand Number
~R4 billion Estimated private investment in backup and alternative energy infrastructure across Ekurhuleni's industrial sector, 2021–2024

Across the three peak years of load-shedding disruption, Ekurhuleni's industrial businesses are estimated to have invested in the region of R4 billion in private backup and alternative energy capacity — generators, solar arrays, battery storage, inverter systems, and the service infrastructure that surrounds them. This figure, necessarily approximate given the informal character of much of the investment, does not include the residential sector or the township commercial economy, whose combined investment would be significant. What it does include is a capital deployment, at private expense, that built a distributed energy infrastructure of a kind no utility has managed to build with public funds in the same timeframe. The crisis funded the transformation. The transformation outlasted the crisis. The R4 billion is not gone. It is on the rooftops.

6. Closing Reflection
The Infrastructure of Necessity

There is a version of the load-shedding story that is purely one of damage: lost production hours, spoiled inventory, businesses that closed, investment decisions that went elsewhere. That version is true. But it is incomplete, because it does not account for what was built in the same period — by the same people, in the same facilities, using the same capital that was originally budgeted for production rather than for energy resilience. Ekurhuleni's parallel grid is not a consolation prize. It is a genuine infrastructure asset: distributed, adaptive, owned outright by the businesses it serves, and entirely immune to the billing disputes and supply interruptions that characterise the formal utility relationship. The next crisis will find this region better prepared than the last one did. Not because of a policy. Because of the inverter on the wall and the panels on the roof and the driver who knows your tank size and your schedule and your phone number, and has never once let you run dry.
