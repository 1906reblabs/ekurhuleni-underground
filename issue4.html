EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 4

The Backyard Economy

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Backyard Economy is not a story about informal housing. It is a story about capital accumulation — about how Ekurhuleni's township households have been quietly building wealth through a shadow property market that operates at city scale, follows its own price signals, and intermediates more rental income annually than most economists have thought to measure.

The deep story: how the backyard room — the single-room structure added behind the main house on an RDP or council plot in Katlehong, Thokoza, Vosloorus, and Duduza — has become the foundational unit of the East Rand's informal property economy. Across hundreds of thousands of township plots, homeowners who received their stands through the post-apartheid housing programme have steadily converted backyard space into rental income, building not just rooms but an entire economy of construction materials, building labour, property management, and household credit that the formal real estate sector has never mapped and the banking system has never adequately financed.

Three supporting micro-stories: the building materials supply chain threading from Brakpan's hardware wholesalers into Katlehong's residential streets, where small-scale builders buy on weekly credit accounts that function as a de facto construction finance system; the emergence of informal property managers in Thokoza and Duduza who maintain the rental books, mediate tenant disputes, and collect payments for absent landlords — running what are effectively small property management firms without the designation; and the rent-to-income lending market in Vosloorus, where mashonisas have begun treating a landlord's rental ledger as collateral, advancing cash against expected monthly receipts.

The overlooked place: the Katlehong residential grid east of Nkosi Street — a dense patchwork of main houses and backyard structures where, on the average residential plot, the backyard accommodation has long since exceeded the main house in total floor area and often in rental yield.

The interesting statistic: an estimated 30% of Ekurhuleni's residential population lives in backyard accommodation — a figure that, if translated into a standalone housing market, would make the backyard sector one of the largest landlord economies in Gauteng by unit count, operating almost entirely outside the formal property registry.

Weekly Issue — 13 February 2026

The Landlords Nobody Counted

How the East Rand's backyard room became the foundational unit of a shadow property economy — and what the wealth it generates reveals about who is actually building Ekurhuleni from the ground up.

1. Editorial
What the Aerial View Misses

Look at a satellite image of Katlehong and the first thing you notice is the density. Not the density of a city centre — the vertical compression of towers and office blocks — but a lateral density, a tightly packed horizontal spread of rooftops that extends further than the eye expects, broken only by the occasional school ground or church yard. Look more carefully, and a second thing becomes apparent: the plots are not uniform. Many of them contain two structures, sometimes three, arranged in a pattern that the original urban planners never drew. A main house occupying roughly a third of the plot. Behind it, separated by a strip of yard, one or two additional structures: smaller, lower, built in a different material or a different era, but clearly permanent, clearly occupied, clearly generating something for the household that owns the land beneath them.

These are backyard rooms. Depending on the part of Ekurhuleni you are looking at, they go by different names — zozo, kaya, room-to-let — but the economic logic beneath them is consistent. A household received a plot through the government's post-apartheid housing programme, or purchased a council house at subsidised price, or inherited a stand that had been occupied since the township was first laid out. The plot came with a main dwelling. The main dwelling, over time, did not exhaust the economic potential of the land. So the household built. A single room at first, perhaps, for an extended family member. Then another, as the demand became clear. Then a small block, four rooms sharing an ablution facility, each let to a working adult or a young family at a monthly rent that, taken together, exceeds what the original house itself could generate.

This is not a marginal phenomenon. It is, in aggregate, one of the largest property markets in Ekurhuleni by unit count — larger than the formal rental sector in most of the townships this publication covers, larger than the combined student accommodation supply of several provincial cities. And it is almost entirely invisible in the data that economists and planners use to understand how the East Rand functions. The formal property registry does not record the backyard rooms. The municipal valuation rolls do not capture their rental income. The banking system does not finance their construction. They exist in the statistics only as a residual — in the gap between the housing units the census counts and the households the census finds.

This issue is an attempt to bring that gap into focus. Not to measure it precisely — the data does not yet exist to do so — but to describe the economy that lives inside it: the construction chains, the informal property managers, the credit relationships, and the landlord class that four decades of backyard building have quietly produced across the East Rand's townships.

2. The Long Essay
The Plot, the Room, and the Rent
Section I — The Observation

Walk any residential street in Katlehong, Thokoza, or Duduza on a weekday morning, and the evidence of the backyard economy is everywhere once you know what to look for. The fresh cement render on a new room addition, still pale against the older brickwork of the main house. The neatly painted doors numbered one through four along a shared passage — numbers that correspond to no municipal address but that are perfectly legible to the tenants who pay rent to use them. The landlady in the front yard sweeping the path between her house and the rooms behind it, the path that is, in effect, the central axis of a small rental business she has been running for fifteen years without ever describing herself as a property developer.

The backyard room economy did not appear suddenly. It grew in the interstices of formal housing policy, in the gap between the government's ability to provide formal housing at scale and the population's need to live somewhere affordable and accessible to work. RDP houses, delivered at pace through the 1990s and 2000s, came with plots large enough to accommodate additional structures. The original beneficiaries — people who had in many cases waited years for a formal dwelling — quickly understood that the plot was not just a place to live. It was an asset. And assets, in the township context, have been monetised with considerable ingenuity.

The backyard room is not a housing problem to be solved. It is a property market to be understood — one that has been building household wealth across the East Rand for thirty years without appearing in a single economic development report.
Section II — The Hidden System

The backyard economy of the East Rand operates through three interconnected systems, each invisible to the formal sector and each more sophisticated than its setting suggests.

The first is the construction supply chain. Building a backyard room in Katlehong or Thokoza is not a simple transaction. It requires materials — bricks, cement, steel lintels, corrugated roofing, timber for window frames — that arrive through a supply chain calibrated to the affordability constraints of the township builder. The hardware wholesalers of Brakpan and the building material dealers along the Springs Road corridor have developed informal credit arrangements with township builders that function as a construction finance system: a builder takes materials on a weekly account, finishes a room, receives the first month's rent from the new tenant, and settles the account. The cycle then repeats. No bank underwrites this credit. No formal contractor manages the project. The credit flows through personal relationship and accumulated trust — the same builder who has settled his accounts reliably for three years can extend his line; the one who has defaulted cannot. This is a credit scoring system. It is simply not a digital one.

The second system is the informal property management economy. As backyard landlords age, or migrate for work, or accumulate enough units that the administration becomes burdensome, a market for management services has emerged. Informal property managers — operating in Thokoza, Duduza, and the older sections of Vosloorus — maintain rental ledgers, collect monthly payments, mediate disputes between tenants sharing ablution facilities, arrange emergency repairs, and remit net rental income to the absent owner via mobile money transfer. They charge a percentage of gross rental collected, typically between eight and twelve percent. They hold no estate agency registration. They carry no professional indemnity insurance. What they do carry is an intimate knowledge of the local rental market — which rooms command a premium for proximity to the taxi rank, which blocks have persistent maintenance problems, which tenants have reliable income and which do not. That knowledge, built over years of daily contact, is the asset the informal property manager sells. It is not replicable by any PropTech platform that has yet launched in this market.

The third system is the rental-income lending market. Across the East Rand's backyard economy, a category of lender has emerged that occupies a space somewhere between the traditional mashonisa and a mortgage provider: operators who advance cash to backyard landlords against the security of their expected rental income. The landlord who has twelve rooms each generating R1,200 per month has a cash flow of R14,400 — predictable, monthly, secured by the physical asset of the rooms themselves. This cash flow can be capitalised. The rental-income lender advances two or three months' worth of expected rent, charges a fee, and collects repayment from the rental receipts as they arrive. The effective cost of this credit is high. But the alternative — a formal personal loan from a bank that does not recognise backyard rental income as serviceability evidence — is unavailable. The rental-income lenders have identified and filled an absence in the formal financial system, as informal operators in this economy consistently do.

Section III — The Broader Insight

What the backyard economy reveals about Ekurhuleni is something that housing economists have been slow to accept: the township housing market is not merely a site of consumption. It is a site of production — of rental income, of construction employment, of credit circulation, and of the kind of incremental asset accumulation that economists recognise as wealth creation when it happens in the formal sector and tend to overlook when it happens in corrugated iron and face brick behind a council house.

The household that has, over twenty years, built eight backyard rooms on an RDP plot in Katlehong owns a productive asset. The rooms generate income. The income compounds: some of it goes back into maintenance and expansion, some into the household's daily needs, some into children's school fees, some into the stokvels and savings clubs that circulate capital further through the township economy. This is not subsistence. This is accumulation. It is simply accumulation that the formal economic framework was not designed to see, because it happens at a scale and in a form — cash transactions, informal tenancies, unregistered structures — that the formal system's instruments are not calibrated to detect.

The policy implication is significant. If the backyard economy is the largest informal wealth-creation mechanism in Ekurhuleni's township areas — and the evidence increasingly suggests that it is — then supporting it should be a priority of economic development policy in the same way that supporting small business formation is a priority. The tools required are not complicated: a simplified process for registering backyard structures and formalising tenancy agreements; a banking product that accepts rental ledgers as evidence of income for credit purposes; a building materials credit guarantee scheme that extends the informal credit chains that already exist. None of these interventions requires dismantling the informal system. They require recognising it clearly enough to extend it wisely.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The backyard rental economy has demonstrated, across three decades of South African economic volatility, a resilience that the formal property market cannot match. During the 2008 financial crisis, formal property values fell sharply and formal rental vacancies rose. Backyard rental demand in Ekurhuleni's townships held steady, because the population seeking affordable accommodation close to work does not disappear when the economy contracts — it grows. During the COVID-19 lockdowns, formal landlords in the middle-income market faced extended payment holidays and legal constraints on eviction. Backyard landlords and their tenants renegotiated directly, informally, and quickly: short-term deferrals, partial payments, work-in-lieu arrangements. The negotiations were often painful. They were also fast, and the tenancy relationships largely survived.

This resilience is structural, not accidental. The backyard rental market is resilient because it serves demand that does not go away — the need for affordable accommodation near employment — at price points the formal sector cannot reach, with flexibility the formal sector cannot offer. A market that serves permanent, inelastic demand at the right price is, in the most precise sense, antifragile: the conditions that damage its formal competitors — rising formal rents, credit tightening, economic contraction — tend to increase the demand it serves. The backyard landlord who has seen three economic cycles knows this intuitively. The economist who has not looked at the data closely enough may not.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Weekend Builders
Katlehong / Thokoza Residential Streets

A loose network of construction teams — typically four to six men, working under a lead builder who handles client relationships and materials procurement — moves through Katlehong and Thokoza on a project-by-project basis, building backyard rooms to a spec that has been refined through hundreds of iterations: a concrete block structure, three metres by four, steel roof, single window, pre-hung door frame, internal plaster and paint. They quote by room rather than by rate, work almost exclusively on weekends and public holidays when the lead builder's regular employment permits, and carry their tools in the boot of a car. Their materials arrive from one of three building supply dealers in Brakpan who know them by name and extend the weekly credit that makes the whole arrangement viable. A team of this kind might complete eight to twelve rooms in a good month. They do not advertise. Their work advertises for them, in the testimonials of landlords whose rooms are consistently occupied.

2. The Ledger Keepers
Thokoza / Duduza Residential Precincts

In the older sections of Thokoza and Duduza, a small number of individuals have built informal property management practices that handle between twenty and sixty rental units each. Their tools are a notebook, a mobile phone with a money transfer app, and a memory for tenant history that most formal property managers would find intimidating. They know which tenants are reliable payers and which require a visit on the first of the month. They know which rooms have a damp problem in winter and which landlord has authorised expenditure on repairs below a certain threshold. They negotiate rent increases, handle utility disputes, and arrange the informal lease renewals — typically a handshake and a verbal confirmation of the new monthly rate — that govern most tenancies in their books. They earn between R1,500 and R3,500 per month from their combined portfolio management fees. It is, by any definition, a professional service. It simply has not been named as one.

3. The Rental-Book Lenders
Vosloorus Extended / Katlehong South

In the sections of Vosloorus and southern Katlehong where backyard rental portfolios have grown largest — plots with eight, ten, sometimes twelve rooms — a category of lender has emerged that treats the rental book as a financial instrument. They advance two to three months' equivalent of a landlord's expected rental income — R15,000 to R40,000 in the cases this publication is aware of — in exchange for a fixed fee and collection rights on the incoming rents until the advance is recovered. The fee structures vary, but the effective monthly cost of this credit is typically between five and ten percent of the principal — high by formal standards, competitive by the standards of what is otherwise available to a borrower whose income is informal and whose primary asset is unregistered. The landlords who use these lenders are not financially naive. They use the advances to fund the next room, to settle a large repair, to cover school fees in January. The credit circulates into productive use. It simply never appears in any formal lending data.

4. The Map of the Week
The Katlehong Backyard Belt, East of Nkosi Street
26°19′S  28°9′E

Approximately three square kilometres. That is the rough footprint of the residential grid in eastern Katlehong where backyard accommodation density is highest — a zone of closely packed plots where the ratio of backyard structures to main houses has, in many streets, exceeded one-to-one. In those three square kilometres you will find: the established landlord households who have been building and managing rooms for fifteen or twenty years; the newer entrants who received their plots more recently and are building their first room with materials bought on credit from a Brakpan supplier; and the informal property manager who moves between both groups, maintaining the rental ledgers of whichever landlords are too busy, too old, or too far away to manage their own books.

What you will not find is a single estate agent's board, a single formal lease agreement in a filing cabinet, or a single room that appears in the municipal valuation roll at its actual rental value. The backyard belt of eastern Katlehong is a functioning property market. It is simply a property market that the formal economy has not yet found a language for. To walk its streets on the first weekend of the month — when rents are collected and builders are at work and the sound of a cement mixer is simply part of the ambient Saturday — is to understand that wealth creation in Ekurhuleni is considerably more widespread, and considerably more self-organised, than any official account suggests.

5. The East Rand Number
~30% Estimated share of Ekurhuleni's residential population living in backyard accommodation — a market that does not exist in the formal property registry

Approximately 30% of Ekurhuleni's residential population — by the most credible estimates derived from census data and household surveys — lives in backyard accommodation: rooms, structures, and converted outbuildings on plots owned by others. At Ekurhuleni's total population of approximately 3.5 million, this translates to more than a million people housed in a market that no estate agent lists, no bank values, and no municipal plan formally governs. The monthly rental income flowing through this market — at an average of R1,000 to R1,500 per room, across hundreds of thousands of units — is a figure that dwarfs most formal retail and commercial property categories in the municipality by rental turnover. It is simply not counted. Numbers that are not counted do not thereby cease to matter.

6. Closing Reflection
The Asset in Plain Sight

There is a particular kind of invisibility that attaches to things that are too large and too ordinary to register as remarkable. The backyard room economy of the East Rand is invisible in this way — not because it hides, but because it is everywhere, woven into the texture of every residential street in every township this publication covers, so familiar to the people who live within it that it requires no explanation, and so unfamiliar to the analysts who study the region from above that it requires no consideration. That gap — between the system as it is lived and the system as it is measured — is the gap this publication exists to close. The landlords of Katlehong and Thokoza and Duduza have been building an economy, room by room, for thirty years. It is time someone wrote it down.

— Ekurhuleni Underground, 13 February 2026
