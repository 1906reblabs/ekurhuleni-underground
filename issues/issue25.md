## THEME ANALYSIS — ISSUE 25

The theme of the Wadeville Succession Line is not a story about retirement. It is a story about a transmission mechanism — apprentice watches master, master corrects apprentice, over years, until the apprentice becomes the next master — that built much of the East Rand's informal repair and fabrication capacity, and that has, across the past decade, quietly stopped refilling itself at the rate it is emptying.

**The deep story:** how the informal master-apprentice lineages that trained a generation of the East Rand's motor rewinders, panel beaters, and general fabricators — the same knowledge systems this publication has traced through Katlehong, Duduza, and Benoni — are ageing out simultaneously, in a workshop belt where the median master is within a decade of retirement, without a formal system positioned to replace the relationship that once did the actual work of transmission.

**Three supporting micro-stories:** the winding-room masters of Wadeville, whose armature-rewinding knowledge has no institutional documentation and whose search for a successor has, for the first time in the trade's local history, moved outside family and neighbourhood referral; the knowledge brokers — former apprentices, now middle-aged — who move between ageing workshops matching younger candidates to masters on terms the old multi-year apprenticeship model never offered; and the panel beaters of the old Germiston-Alberton motor trade strip who are choosing to close rather than sell, because the skill and client trust that make the business valuable cannot be transferred in a single transaction.

**The overlooked place:** Wadeville, the light-industrial township east of Germiston's main rail siding, where post-war industrial expansion concentrated small mechanical and electrical trade workshops from the 1960s onward.

**The interesting statistic:** an estimated 40.7% of South Africa's experienced artisans are approaching retirement, against a youth pipeline industry bodies describe as too thin to replace them — a demographic reality playing out, workshop by workshop, across Ekurhuleni's informal trade economy.

### 1. Editorial

**The Sound the Winding Room Makes**

In a shed on the eastern edge of Wadeville, the sound that has filled the mornings for forty-one years is the specific hum of a coil winder pulling copper wire onto an armature core, followed by a pause while the winder checks the tension by feel rather than by gauge. The man doing it is sixty-eight. He has rewound motors for pumps, compressors, and conveyor drives across three decades of Ekurhuleni's industrial belt, judging insulation failure by smell and winding faults by the particular resistance of the wire under his thumb. He has no apprentice. He has had three in the past six years, each of whom left within eighteen months for work that paid weekly rather than after years of unpaid observation.

I have been thinking about what happens to a trade when the knowledge inside it is real, valuable, and entirely undocumented — held only in the hands and habits of people who are, on average and by most industry accounts, now in their late fifties. This is not a story about a single stubborn man refusing to modernise. It is a story about an entire transmission mechanism — apprentice watches master, master corrects apprentice, over years, until the apprentice becomes the next master — that built much of the East Rand's informal repair and fabrication capacity, and that has, across the past decade, quietly stopped refilling itself at the rate it is emptying.

This publication has, across several issues, described the workshops this transmission mechanism produced: the toolmakers of Benoni, the artisan lineages of Duduza, the hydraulic rebuilders and gearbox specialists of Katlehong. What none of those issues addressed directly is what happens next — not to the workshops themselves, which continue operating for now, but to the knowledge inside them, once the specific person holding it retires, emigrates, or simply stops being able to do the physically demanding work the trade requires.

Wadeville is a useful place to watch this unfold, because its concentration of motor rewinding, panel beating, and general fabrication workshops is old enough that the succession question has arrived for most of them within the same few years. What is happening there is not collapse. It is something quieter and, in its way, more interesting: an informal market in knowledge transfer that has emerged specifically to solve the problem the formal training system has not solved, run by people who have understood, faster than any policy document, that an apprenticeship was never a course. It is a relationship, and relationships take years to build and only one retirement to lose.

### 2. The Long Essay

**The Trade That Has No Certificate for What It Knows**

**Section I — The Observation**

Walk the workshop rows east of Wadeville's main rail siding on a weekday morning and the age of the trade announces itself before anything else does. The men at the winding benches, the panel-beating bays, and the general fabrication sheds are, disproportionately, older than fifty — grey-haired, moving with the particular economy of motion that comes from having done the same physical task for four decades. Younger workers are present, but mostly as general labour: loading, cleaning, running parts between bays. The specific skilled work — reading an armature fault, matching a panel's curve by eye, judging a weld's penetration by the sound of the arc — is still, in most of these workshops, performed by one person, because that person has not yet found, trained, or trusted anyone else to do it.

**Section II — The Hidden System**

The succession economy of Wadeville's workshop belt operates through three layers, each responding to a different part of the transmission problem the old master-apprentice system has stopped solving on its own.

The first layer is the ageing masters themselves — the winding-room specialists, panel beaters, and fabricators whose skills were transmitted to them, in most cases, by a father, uncle, or long-term employer across years of unpaid or low-paid observation. That transmission model assumed a supply of young men willing to trade several years of low income for eventual mastery — an assumption that held for decades and has, over the past ten to fifteen years, stopped holding. Formal industry data on South Africa's artisan population confirms what these workshop owners describe anecdotally: the average working artisan in the country is now around fifty-five years old, and a substantial share of the country's experienced tradespeople are expected to retire within a horizon the youth pipeline is not currently sized to fill. In Wadeville, this arrives not as a national statistic but as a specific and repeated experience: three apprentices, each gone within eighteen months, for a workshop that once could rely on informal referral through family and neighbourhood networks to supply a steady trickle of willing learners.

The second layer is the knowledge broker — a role that did not exist in the trade a generation ago and has emerged specifically to address the transmission gap. These are typically former apprentices themselves, now in their thirties or forties, who move between several ageing workshops on an informal, fee-for-introduction basis: documenting technique on their phones with the master's permission, identifying younger workers with genuine aptitude and patience, and structuring shorter, more flexible arrangements than the old multi-year apprenticeship — a paid trial period, a defined skills target, a realistic timeline — that a modern young worker will actually accept. They are not licensed. They are not recognised by any training authority. They are, in the workshops that have used them successfully, the only reason a specific piece of technical knowledge did not simply retire with the person who held it.

The third layer is outright closure — the workshops whose owners, unable to find a successor through either the old channel or the new broker-mediated one, have chosen to wind down rather than sell. A motor rewinding business or a panel-beating shop is not, in most cases, a saleable asset in the way a retail business is: its value is inseparable from the specific skill and client trust of the person running it, and a buyer who lacks that skill inherits premises and equipment but not the thing that actually generated income. Several workshop owners in Wadeville describe this directly: they would rather close than sell to someone who will fail within a year and take the workshop's reputation down with them.

> *An apprenticeship was never a course. It was a relationship — and a relationship takes years to build and only one retirement to lose.*

**Section III — The Broader Insight**

What the Wadeville succession crisis reveals about Ekurhuleni's informal economy is a vulnerability this publication has not previously named directly, though it runs beneath several of the systems already described: informal knowledge economies are exceptionally good at solving problems the formal system does not address, and exceptionally bad at solving the specific problem of their own continuity, because the transmission mechanism that built them was never designed as a system. It was a series of individual relationships — father to son, master to the neighbour's boy who showed promise — that happened to aggregate, across decades, into something that looked systemic from the outside. It was never actually systemic. It was personal, all the way down, which means it can end the same way it began: one relationship at a time, and one relationship not formed is one specific body of knowledge gone.

This matters for how Ekurhuleni thinks about its industrial base going forward. The region's informal repair and fabrication capacity — documented across this publication's coverage of Katlehong, Benoni, and Duduza — is not a fixed asset that will simply persist because it has persisted for decades. It is a knowledge stock that depreciates the moment its holders retire without having transmitted it, and the depreciation is currently outpacing the transmission. No skills programme currently operating in the region is calibrated to this specific problem, because the problem is not a shortage of training capacity. It is a shortage of the particular, patient, years-long relationship that training capacity was never designed to replicate.

**Section IV — The Antifragile Angle**

The knowledge-broker layer of the Wadeville succession economy has a genuinely antifragile relationship to the crisis it addresses: it did not exist when the old apprenticeship system was working, and it has grown directly in proportion to that system's failure. Every workshop owner who cannot find a successor through family or neighbourhood referral becomes, immediately, a potential client for a broker who can offer an alternative channel. Every young worker who is interested in a trade but unwilling to accept the old system's unpaid multi-year terms becomes, immediately, a potential candidate the broker can place on better terms. The worse the formal succession crisis becomes, the more valuable the broker's specific service — matching, structuring, translating between a master's expectations and a young worker's constraints — becomes to both sides of the relationship.

This is a genuinely new economic role, not an adaptation of an old one, and its emergence is itself informative: when a knowledge transmission system fails at scale, the market does not simply lose the knowledge. It generates intermediaries whose entire function is closing the specific gap the failure created. The brokers of Wadeville did not exist fifteen years ago because they were not needed fifteen years ago. They exist now because the crisis that makes their service valuable is real, ongoing, and — on current demographic evidence — likely to deepen before it eases.

### 3. The Hidden Economy

**Three Operations Nobody Is Watching**

**1. The Winding-Room Masters**
WADEVILLE INDUSTRIAL TOWNSHIP, GERMISTON

Along Wadeville's motor rewinding row, a cluster of specialists in their sixties and seventies still perform the trade's most technical work personally: diagnosing armature faults by smell and sound, rewinding coils to a tension judged by feel rather than gauge, and testing insulation resistance against decades of accumulated intuition about what a healthy reading looks like for a given motor's age and duty cycle. None of this is written down. Several took on apprentices through family or neighbourhood referral for most of their careers; in the past six years, that channel has largely stopped producing candidates willing to accept years of low-paid observation before the skill pays off, leaving veteran rewinders searching, for the first time, outside the networks that always used to supply them.

**2. The Knowledge Brokers**
WADEVILLE / ALRODE WORKSHOP FRINGE

A small, informal cohort of former apprentices — now in their thirties and forties, trained under masters who are themselves nearing retirement — has built a matchmaking role that did not exist in the trade a generation ago. They move between ageing workshops with the master's trust already established, documenting technique on video with permission, identifying younger workers with genuine aptitude, and structuring shorter, better-paid arrangements than the old apprenticeship model that a modern candidate will actually accept. They charge an informal introduction or retainer fee, paid by whichever workshop successfully places a candidate. Their value is entirely relational: trusted by the generation retiring and legible to the generation replacing it, a translation service neither side can provide for itself.

**3. The Quiet Closures**
OLD MOTOR TRADE STRIP, GERMISTON–ALBERTON BORDER

Along the older panel-beating strip straddling the Germiston-Alberton boundary, several workshop owners approaching retirement have chosen to wind down operations entirely rather than sell. The reasoning is consistent across cases: a panel-beating business's value lies almost entirely in the proprietor's hand-eye judgment and long-standing client trust, neither of which transfers with a change of ownership, and a buyer who lacks both will typically fail within a year, damaging the workshop's reputation in a tight-knit trade network in the process. Several owners describe preferring closure — selling equipment individually, releasing the lease — to watching a name they built over decades attached to work they no longer control.

### 4. The Map of the Week

**The Wadeville Motor Trade Row, Germiston**

*26°16′S  28°11′E*

Roughly two kilometres. That is the working length of Wadeville's motor rewinding and general fabrication row, a light-industrial strip east of the main rail siding where Germiston's post-war industrial expansion concentrated small mechanical and electrical trade workshops from the 1960s onward. In those two kilometres you will find: winding rooms where the specialist work is still performed by a single ageing proprietor; panel-beating bays whose order books remain full even as their succession plans remain empty; and, moving between them on an informal daily circuit, the knowledge brokers who have made introducing masters to candidates into an unofficial trade of their own.

What you will not find is any signage indicating which workshops are searching for a successor and which have quietly decided not to. That information circulates by word of mouth, through the same broker network that is trying to solve the problem it depends on for its own relevance. To walk this row on a weekday morning, and to notice how few of the hands doing the specialist work are young, is to understand that Ekurhuleni's informal industrial capacity has an expiry mechanism built into it that no inventory of workshops, machines, or order books will ever show.

### 5. The East Rand Number

**40.7%**

**Estimated share of South Africa's experienced artisans approaching retirement, against a youth pipeline industry bodies describe as too thin to replace them**

Industry analyses cited by South Africa's technical training sector put the share of the country's experienced artisan workforce approaching retirement at roughly 40.7%, against a national artisan population whose average age is now estimated at around fifty-five. The Steel and Engineering Industries Federation of South Africa has separately noted a steep decline in new entrants to artisanal learning programmes and describes the resulting shortfall as a twenty-year gap in the pipeline. In Wadeville, this arrives as an individual and specific fact, one retiring winder at a time — a demographic pattern that shows up, workshop by workshop, well before it shows up in any provincial skills audit.

---

### 6. Closing Reflection

**What Retires With Him**

The winder in Wadeville will keep working for a few more years, by his own estimate, before his hands stop being reliable enough for the close work the trade demands. He has, as of this writing, no successor. He has had three broker-arranged introductions in the past year, one of which is showing promise — a twenty-six-year-old who has stayed four months longer than any previous apprentice and asks, he says, better questions than he did at that age. Whether that relationship survives long enough to transfer four decades of accumulated judgment is not yet knowable. What is knowable is what happens if it does not: not a gap in a database, but a specific, particular kind of knowing that simply stops existing the day he does.
