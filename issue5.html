EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 5

The Taxi Republic

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Taxi Republic is not a story about minibuses. It is a story about governance — about how a privately organised, route-based transport economy built its own regulatory architecture, its own capital markets, and its own territorial logic across the East Rand, decades before the formal public transport system began trying to understand what it had missed.

The deep story: how Ekurhuleni's taxi associations — the route committees, the rank marshals, the association treasuries — function as a parallel municipal system, allocating rights, enforcing obligations, collecting levies, and mediating disputes across a network that moves more people daily than any Gauteng Transit system has ever managed. The association is not the mob it is often caricatured as. It is, in the specific sense that urban economists mean, an institution — one with rules, sanctions, succession, and a theory of value.

Three supporting micro-stories: the route permit economy along the Katlehong-Johannesburg corridor, where operating rights are informally traded at valuations that reflect years of accumulated ridership; the rank-adjacent financial services cluster at Vosloorus's main taxi rank, where insurance brokers, informal lenders, and panel beaters have co-located around the concentration of driver income; and the taxi industry's secondary logistics function across Daveyton and Tsakane, where the same vehicles that carry commuters in the morning carry light goods — produce, electronics, clothing consignments — on the return legs that would otherwise run empty.

The overlooked place: the Vosloorus Main Rank — a node that processes tens of thousands of passenger movements daily, anchors a dense commercial precinct, and serves as the financial clearing house for one of the busiest informal transport corridors in the East Rand.

The interesting statistic: the minibus taxi industry nationally carries an estimated 15 million passenger trips per day — more than all other forms of public transport in South Africa combined. In Ekurhuleni, where Metrobus and Gautrain reach only a fraction of the population, the taxi network is not a supplement to the public transport system. It is the public transport system.

Weekly Issue — 20 February 2026

The Taxi Republic

How Ekurhuleni's minibus taxi network built its own economy, its own governance, and its own version of the city — and why the formal transport system has never caught up.

1. Editorial
Five O'Clock in the East Rand

By five o'clock in the morning, the rank is already busy. Not busy in the way a shopping centre becomes busy — the gradual accumulation of individuals making independent decisions — but busy in the way a port is busy: purposeful, sequential, organised by roles that everyone present understands without being told. The marshal directs the queue. The loader handles the door. The driver calls his destination in a shorthand developed over decades of use. The passengers sort themselves by direction with the practiced efficiency of people who have done this every weekday for years. Within ninety seconds of arriving, a vehicle that was empty is full and gone. Within thirty seconds of that, the next one has taken its place.

I have been watching taxi ranks for some time now, in the way you watch something that is simultaneously mundane and extraordinary. The mundane part is obvious: this is the morning commute, the unremarkable daily churn of labour moving from where it sleeps to where it works. The extraordinary part takes longer to see. What you are watching, if you look carefully, is not transport. You are watching a system — a privately governed, economically sophisticated, territorially defined system that moves approximately a million people a day across Ekurhuleni without a single municipal subsidy, without a public timetable, and without the kind of institutional support that every formal transport operator in the country depends on as a matter of course.

The minibus taxi industry is the most written-about and least understood sector of the South African informal economy. It is written about mostly as a problem: dangerous, unregulated, resistant to reform, periodically violent. All of those things are, in different degrees at different times, true. But they are not the most interesting things about it. The most interesting thing is what the taxi industry actually is, stripped of the problem frame: a sophisticated private governance system that has, over forty years, built a working transport network across the most economically complex metropolitan geography in sub-Saharan Africa. That it has done this without being asked, without being funded, and largely without being understood is what makes it worth examining as a publication focused on hidden systems.

This issue is about the taxi republic of the East Rand — not as a transport story, but as an economic and institutional one. What does the taxi network actually govern? How does it allocate value? What financial system runs alongside it? And what has grown up in its orbit that nobody thinks to count when they tally Ekurhuleni's economic assets?

2. The Long Essay
Routes, Rights, and the Republic of the Rank
Section I — The Observation

Every taxi route in Ekurhuleni is, in the formal sense, a licensed operating right: a permission granted by the regulatory authority to carry passengers between two defined points. In theory this permission is non-transferable, tied to a specific vehicle, subject to renewal, and revocable for cause. In practice — in the actual economic life of the industry — the route permit is an asset. It is bought and sold, inherited and pledged, priced by the market and appreciated or depreciated by the same forces that move any property value: demand, competition, the economic health of the communities it connects. A permit for a well-travelled route between Katlehong and the Johannesburg CBD carries a market value that no official document acknowledges but that every taxi operator and every bank that lends to them understands perfectly well.

This is where the taxi industry's parallel institutional architecture begins. The associations — SANTACO affiliates in Ekurhuleni's case, organised at the route and sub-route level — are the institutions that recognise, protect, and adjudicate these property rights. They are also the institutions that allocate loading slots at the ranks, manage the queue discipline that makes the five o'clock rank function, collect levies that fund association operations, and mediate the disputes that arise whenever the value of a route is contested. They do all of this without formal legal standing in most of what they actually govern. Their authority derives not from statute but from the consent of the operators who depend on the system they run — and from the credible threat, periodically demonstrated, that the consent can be withdrawn and the system can be shut down.

The taxi association does not govern transport. It governs territory — and territory is the oldest form of economic value there is.
Section II — The Hidden System

The hidden economy of the East Rand taxi network operates through three systems that sit largely invisible below the level of the vehicles themselves.

The first is the route permit market. A working route between Katlehong and the Johannesburg CBD — one of the busiest corridors in Ekurhuleni — represents a revenue stream: fifteen, twenty, sometimes thirty trips a day at a fixed fare, multiplied by a load factor that experienced operators know down to the time of day and day of the week. That revenue stream can be capitalised. Operators borrow against it. They sell it when they retire. They pass it to children who inherit not just a vehicle but a business with a defensible market position. The banks — specifically the vehicle finance divisions of the major commercial banks — have been lending against taxi assets for years, which means they have been implicitly recognising the value of the route rights those assets generate. This is not informal finance in the sense of being improvised. It is informal governance giving rise to formal capital markets. The route permit market is where the two systems meet.

The second hidden system is the rank precinct economy. Around any significant taxi rank in Ekurhuleni — Vosloorus Main, the Katlehong interchange, the Daveyton rank on the road to Benoni — a dense commercial ecosystem has developed that is economically dependent on the concentration of driver income and passenger footfall. Panel beaters and tyre shops cluster near the rank because that is where the vehicles are. Informal lenders operate at the rank perimeter because drivers, who are often owner-operators managing daily cash flows, have irregular but predictable credit needs. Insurance brokers work the rank because the taxi finance agreement requires insurance and because drivers buy it where they park. The food vendors, the airtime sellers, the clothing traders — all of them have calibrated their operations to the rhythms of the rank in the same way that the Germiston station traders calibrated theirs to the train timetable. The rank is not just a transport node. It is a commercial anchor.

The third system is the freight function — the least visible and, in some respects, the most economically interesting. On the return legs of long-distance commuter routes, and on the shorter routes connecting township residential areas to commercial centres, taxi vehicles routinely carry goods as well as passengers. This is not formalised. It is not priced by tariff. It exists because a vehicle that is returning largely empty represents an economic opportunity that the driver — who is typically responsible for his own fuel costs — has every incentive to fill. The result is a dispersed, unscheduled, but consistent last-mile freight service threading through Daveyton, Tsakane, Vosloorus, and Thokoza that moves small consignments — produce from the large fresh markets, electronic goods from the Johannesburg CBD traders, clothing bundles from informal importers — into residential areas that no formal courier service covers economically. It is a freight network that has no name, no timetable, and no commercial registration. It works.

Section III — The Broader Insight

What the taxi republic reveals about Ekurhuleni's economy is a principle that applies far beyond transport: complex economic systems can sustain themselves through private governance when the formal alternative is absent, expensive, or structurally incapable of addressing actual demand. South Africa's public transport planners have spent forty years trying to bring the taxi industry into a regulated framework without quite succeeding, not because the industry is ungovernable but because the formal framework consistently misunderstands what the industry actually governs. It does not govern vehicles. It does not govern timetables. It governs routes — the right to a revenue stream in a defined territory — and the governance of routes requires an institutional architecture that no transport regulator has been willing to recognise as legitimate.

The taxi industry's institutional architecture — the associations, the route committees, the rank hierarchies — is, in the language of institutional economics, a system of private property rights operating in the absence of state enforcement. It is imperfect. Its enforcement mechanisms are sometimes brutal. Its exclusion of new entrants protects incumbents at the cost of innovation. But it is functional: it has sustained a transport network across the East Rand for four decades without subsidy, and it has done so by aligning the incentives of the operators who run it with the interests of the passengers who depend on it. When a route fails — when the demand is insufficient to support the vehicles on it — the operators move. When a new origin-destination pair emerges, because a new township extension has been settled or a new factory has opened, new routes follow. The system is responsive in the way that planned transport systems consistently are not, because it is governed by the people whose income depends on getting it right.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The taxi network of the East Rand has been subjected, over the past decade, to every category of stress that a transport system can face: fuel price volatility, vehicle finance crises, pandemic restrictions that briefly collapsed ridership, the rolling disruption of load-shedding that scrambled morning commute patterns, and the persistent competition of e-hailing platforms that made significant inroads in wealthier commuter markets. It has survived all of them. It has, in several cases, grown stronger through them.

When e-hailing platforms expanded across Gauteng and captured a segment of the middle-income commuter market, the taxi industry did not lose market share in Ekurhuleni in any meaningful sense, because its market — the township commuter who travels fixed routes at fixed times and cannot afford surge pricing — was not the market the platforms were pursuing. The competition clarified rather than threatened. When load-shedding disrupted the formal economy and shifted commute timing unpredictably, taxi operators adjusted their frequency and their pricing within days; no operator waited for a regulatory decision. When the pandemic slashed ridership during lockdowns, operators with diversified income — from the freight function, from the rank-adjacent businesses — survived; those who were purely dependent on passenger fares did not, but the routes they vacated were filled by returning operators within weeks of restrictions lifting. The system contracted sharply and recovered sharply, which is the definition of resilience. It did not break, which is the definition of something more than resilience.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Permit Brokers
Katlehong / Vosloorus Route Corridors

In the informal economy surrounding Ekurhuleni's taxi associations, a small number of permit brokers operate as the intermediaries in the market for route operating rights. They know which routes are financially viable, which permits are held by operators approaching retirement, which associations are open to new membership and which are effectively closed to outsiders. When a buyer wants access to a lucrative corridor and a seller wants to exit, the broker structures the transaction: negotiating the association's approval, arranging the vehicle finance, facilitating the transfer of the operating licence through whatever formal and informal channels the specific route requires. They charge a percentage of the transaction value and operate entirely by referral. They have no office, no website, and no business registration. They have, in some cases, facilitated transactions worth more than the price of the vehicles involved.

2. The Rank Lenders
Vosloorus Main Rank / Katlehong CBD Rank

At the edges of Ekurhuleni's major taxi ranks, a tier of informal short-term lenders has established itself that serves a specific credit need the formal banking system cannot address: the owner-operator driver who runs short of cash for fuel, vehicle maintenance, or association levies mid-week, before the weekend's higher ridership generates the income to cover it. These lenders are not mashonisas in the traditional sense — they are not charging the extreme rates of household microcredit. Their clients are asset-backed operators with daily cash flows; their rates reflect the credit risk accordingly. They know their clients personally, know their vehicles, know their routes, and know the association standing of everyone they lend to. Their default rate, by their own account, is low — not because formal credit scoring has selected for quality, but because social accountability at the rank is total. Everyone knows who repays and who does not.

3. The Return-Leg Traders
Daveyton / Tsakane Township Corridors

On the routes running east from Johannesburg's fresh produce markets and CBD clothing districts into Daveyton and Tsakane, a network of small traders has built a distribution model entirely dependent on the taxi industry's return-leg capacity. They buy in the city in the morning — fresh produce, pre-packaged groceries, electronics accessories, clothing — and ship it home via a network of regular drivers who know the merchandise, know the consignee, and know that a small handling fee is part of every arrangement. The goods move without a waybill, without insurance, and without any formal logistics contract. They arrive at their destination the same morning they were purchased. For the spaza operators and small retailers of Daveyton's residential streets, this is not a logistics service. It is their supply chain.

4. The Map of the Week
Vosloorus Main Rank
26°20′S  28°8′E

Stand at the Vosloorus Main Rank on a weekday morning at half past five, and what you are standing inside is one of the most economically active square kilometres in Ekurhuleni. The rank itself — the loading bays, the queue lanes, the marshal stations — is the obvious centre. But the economic activity extends outward in every direction: the food vendors whose operations are timed to the departure schedule, the phone and airtime stalls that serve both drivers and passengers, the panel beaters and tyre repair shops on the access roads that position themselves for the mechanical trade the vehicle concentration generates, and the insurance and financial services operators who have learned that a driver with a new finance agreement and a working vehicle is a customer worth finding at the rank.

No city planner designed this commercial ecosystem. No investment promotion body seeded it. It grew because the rank created a concentration of income — driver income, passenger income, goods-in-transit income — and concentrations of income always attract the services that can extract value from them. The Vosloorus rank is, in the language of economic geography, an agglomeration. It is simply an agglomeration that the formal economy has not yet thought to count.

5. The East Rand Number
15 million Daily passenger trips carried by South Africa's minibus taxi industry — more than all other public transport modes combined

South Africa's minibus taxi industry carries an estimated 15 million passenger trips per day, accounting for roughly 65% of all public transport journeys in the country. In Ekurhuleni — where Metrobus routes cover only portions of the formal suburban grid and Gautrain serves a narrow, high-income corridor — the taxi share is considerably higher. For the majority of Ekurhuleni's working population, the taxi is not a transport option. It is the only option. An industry that carries this volume, at no cost to the state, and that has built the institutional architecture to sustain itself across four decades of economic turbulence, is not an informal appendage to the transport system. It is the transport system. The informal label is a measurement failure, not a description of scale.

6. Closing Reflection
The Republic That Built Itself

There is a phrase that sometimes appears in South African transport policy documents: "rationalising the taxi industry." It means bringing the industry into a formal regulatory framework — licensing, compliance, subsidy, route planning from above. The phrase has been appearing for thirty years without the rationalisation quite arriving, and the reason is worth sitting with. The taxi industry has not resisted formalisation because it is ungovernable. It has resisted it because it is already governed — by a system it built itself, that serves its members, and that has proven more adaptive than anything the formal sector has offered as a replacement. The question for Ekurhuleni is not how to rationalise this republic. It is how to recognise what the republic has already built, and whether the city that depends on it is prepared to treat it as the asset it is.
