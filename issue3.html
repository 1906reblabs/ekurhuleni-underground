EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 3

The Scrap Economy

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Scrap Economy is not a story about waste. It is a story about a commodity system — one that begins with a man pushing a trolley through the streets of Katlehong at dawn and ends with a container of processed ferrous bale loaded onto a ship at Durban for delivery to a steel mill in Asia. Between those two points, the East Rand's informal and semi-formal scrap metal trade intermediates one of the most significant unmapped commodity flows in inland South Africa.

The deep story: how a tiered, self-organised network of collectors, aggregators, and processors has built a functioning commodity supply chain across Ekurhuleni's township and industrial zones — one that recycles the physical residue of a century of manufacturing and mining into export-grade secondary raw material, entirely without formal coordination, without government support, and without appearing in any economic profile of the region as a strategic asset.

Three supporting micro-stories: the street-level collectors of Katlehong and Vosloorus who work the residential grid daily, sourcing scrap from households, construction sites, and the margins of industrial zones, selling by weight at the neighbourhood buying point; the mid-tier aggregator yards of Boksburg and Germiston that sort, grade, and bale material for onward sale to the large processors and steel mills; and the specialist copper and aluminium strippers operating in the light industrial zones of Brakpan, who extract non-ferrous metals from decommissioned electrical cable and machinery components for sale into a global commodity market that sets their price in dollars before they have weighed the morning's intake.

The overlooked place: the scrap dealer corridor running along the old Cleveland Road axis between Germiston and Boksburg — a three-kilometre stretch of yards, weighbridges, and processing sheds whose combined daily throughput of secondary metal dwarfs most formal manufacturing operations in the immediate vicinity, and which appears on no industrial map of the region.

The interesting statistic: South Africa's formal and informal scrap metal industry is estimated to recycle more than two million tonnes of ferrous and non-ferrous metal annually — a volume that supplies approximately 30% of the country's domestic steel production as secondary raw material. The East Rand, as the country's most concentrated legacy industrial zone, generates a disproportionate share of this material. The collectors who gather it are, in a precise economic sense, the first link in a global manufacturing supply chain.

Weekly Issue — 6 February 2026

The Metal That Never Stops Moving

Inside the East Rand's scrap economy — from the trolley on the Katlehong street to the container at Durban port, and the hidden commodity system that connects them.

1. Editorial
Before the City Wakes

Sometime around five-thirty on any weekday morning, a particular kind of figure appears on the residential streets of Katlehong. He is pushing a trolley — a supermarket trolley, modified with a wooden base and reinforced with wire, or a purpose-built two-wheel cart welded from angle iron by someone who understood the load it would carry. The trolley is already partially loaded: a twist of copper wire here, a length of aluminium angle there, a tangle of steel reinforcing bar salvaged from a construction site the previous afternoon. He is heading for the buying point, a yard behind a corrugated iron fence three streets over, where a hand-painted sign gives a price per kilogram for ferrous, non-ferrous, and electronic scrap and where a diesel-powered weighbridge has been running since before the residential streets around it began to stir.

This scene repeats itself across every township in Ekurhuleni, every morning, without announcement and without any of the participants describing themselves as part of an industry. And yet they are. What the man with the trolley is doing is work — skilled, physically demanding, knowledge-intensive work that requires him to recognise the grade of metal by its colour and weight, to know which construction sites and demolition projects have left material accessible, to understand the daily price movements that will determine whether this morning's load earns R80 or R120. He is, in the precise economic sense, a commodity collector operating in a supply chain that extends from the streets of Katlehong to the steel mills of South Korea and China.

I have been thinking about the scrap economy of the East Rand for some time now, not as a curiosity but as a system. What strikes me is not its scale — though the scale is significant — but its architecture. The scrap trade has organised itself, across decades, into a tiered structure of extraordinary efficiency: collectors feeding aggregators feeding processors feeding mills and exporters, each layer adding value, each layer applying specialised knowledge, none of it coordinated by any institution or mapped by any government agency. It is, in the language of systems thinking, a spontaneous order — a complex functional system that nobody designed and nobody directs, but that processes millions of tonnes of secondary raw material per year with a reliability that most planned supply chains would struggle to match.

This issue is an attempt to trace that system from its first link to its last — and to ask why a commodity supply chain of this significance has remained, for so long, invisible to the analysts and planners who purport to understand how Ekurhuleni's economy works.

2. The Long Essay
The Commodity Chain Nobody Mapped
Section I — The Observation

Drive the old Cleveland Road axis between Germiston and Boksburg on a Tuesday morning and the scrap economy announces itself without subtlety. The yards appear in clusters — ten metres of corrugated iron fence, a gate wide enough for a tipper truck, the compressed silhouette of baled metal visible above the roofline. Between the yards, smaller buying points occupy former service stations and vacant plots, their scales and price boards the only signage. The smell is specific: a mixture of machine oil, oxidised iron, and something else, something mineral and sharp, that you begin to associate with the material rather than with any particular place. At the yards' weighbridges, trucks queue. At the smaller buying points, the trolleys queue. The whole corridor is in motion before most of the surrounding industrial zone has opened its gates.

What is not immediately visible is the hierarchy embedded in this activity. The man with the trolley and the operator of the large processing yard are both part of the same supply chain, but they occupy very different positions within it. Between them — between the street collector and the export-grade processor — there are typically two or three intermediate layers: the neighbourhood buying point, the small aggregator yard, and in some cases a specialist sorter or grader who separates mixed scrap into the commodity classifications that the large processors and mills require. Each layer performs a distinct economic function. Each layer earns a margin on the value it adds. Understanding the scrap economy requires understanding all of them, because the system only works as a system. Any one layer in isolation would be economically inviable.

The man with the trolley is not at the bottom of a waste hierarchy. He is at the beginning of a global supply chain — and he knows it better than the economists who have never described it.
Section II — The Hidden System

The scrap economy of the East Rand operates through four distinct layers, each with its own economics, its own knowledge requirements, and its own relationship to the formal commodity markets that ultimately price the material it handles.

The first layer is the street collector — the trolley operator, the scrap-laden bakkie driver, the household-to-household buyer who works a defined residential territory with the consistency of a postman. These collectors have mapped their territory at a granularity that no geographic information system has replicated: they know which households generate what categories of material and on what rhythm, which construction projects are at a stage where offcuts and off-spec material will be available, which municipal maintenance crews leave pipe and cable remnants accessible. Their knowledge is the most perishable asset in the chain — it degrades if they leave the territory and it cannot be easily transferred — which is why established collectors defend their routes with a seriousness that reflects genuine economic value.

The second layer is the neighbourhood buying point. These are small fixed yards — typically operated from a rented or owner-occupied industrial plot of a few hundred square metres — that buy from collectors, hold mixed material for accumulation, and perform a first-stage sorting. The buying point operator needs to know commodity prices in near-real time, because the price he offers collectors must leave him a margin against what he will receive from the next layer up, and metal prices can move significantly within a single trading day. Several buying point operators in Katlehong and Vosloorus track London Metal Exchange prices via their phones, translating dollar per tonne figures into rand per kilogram at the morning's exchange rate before opening their gates. This is sophisticated commodity trading, executed in a corrugated iron yard with a bathroom scale and a handwritten ledger.

The third layer is the aggregator and grader. These larger yards — the ones visible along the Cleveland Road corridor, with their tipper trucks and overhead cranes and baling presses — buy mixed material from multiple buying points, sort it into commodity-grade categories, and process it into the standardised forms that mills and exporters require: HMS (heavy melting steel) bales, shredded ferrous, cleaned copper cathode-equivalent, aluminium grades. The grading knowledge required at this layer is substantial. The difference between a tonne of HMS 1 and a tonne of HMS 2 is a price difference that runs to hundreds of rands; misclassification in either direction costs money. The large aggregators employ graders whose expertise is accumulated over years and is not easily documented or taught outside of practical apprenticeship.

The fourth layer is the exporter and mill. At the top of the chain, the processed material enters the formal commodity economy: it is weighed, assayed, contracted, and shipped. The large steel mills of Gauteng and the export yards at Durban and Richards Bay are the terminal buyers. What arrives at their gates is a standardised commodity product that could have been produced anywhere in the world. The fact that it began as a trolley-load on a Katlehong street is not legible from the bale.

Section III — The Broader Insight

The scrap economy of the East Rand contains a lesson that urban economists rarely extract from it: informal commodity systems are not peripheral to formal markets. They are, in many cases, the foundation of them. South Africa's domestic steel industry depends on secondary raw material — scrap — to supplement the primary iron ore and coal inputs that conventional steelmaking requires. At current levels of domestic scrap supply, approximately a third of the steel produced in South Africa is made from recycled material. The informal and semi-formal collectors, buying points, and aggregators of the East Rand are responsible for a significant share of that supply. They are, in a functional sense, part of the upstream supply chain of the country's formal steel industry. That the two sectors have never been formally connected — that no procurement relationship, no quality partnership, no supply development programme has linked the Katlehong collector to the Vanderbijlpark mill — is less a commentary on the informal sector's limitations than on the formal sector's failure of imagination.

The broader implication for Ekurhuleni is about recognition. The scrap economy is not a residual. It is not a symptom of poverty or of inadequate formal employment. It is a functioning commodity system that has developed the infrastructure — physical, social, and financial — to handle a significant volume of secondary raw material efficiently and at low cost. The city that recognises this is a city that can begin to think about how to extend the system's reach: how to give aggregator yards better access to finance, how to create grading standards that bridge the informal and formal sectors, how to connect the collector at the bottom of the chain with the market intelligence that currently lives only at the top.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The scrap metal economy is, by its nature, antifragile in the specific sense that its inputs increase when the formal economy deteriorates. Every factory closure, every infrastructure failure, every abandoned industrial site produces raw material for the scrap chain. The wave of deindustrialisation that swept through the East Rand's heavy manufacturing sector in the 1990s and 2000s was, from the perspective of the scrap economy, a supply event. The decommissioning of old mine infrastructure in the Springs corridor continues to generate feedstock. When load-shedding damaged industrial equipment beyond repair, the resulting write-offs flowed into the scrap yards within weeks. The formal economy's loss is, at each of these moments, the informal commodity system's gain.

This dynamic has a counterintuitive implication: the scrap economy is most active, most economically significant, and most socially important precisely at the moments when the formal economy is most distressed. In a recession, the collector's route becomes more valuable because more households are selling material they would otherwise retain. In a wave of industrial restructuring, the aggregator yards fill with decommissioned plant that the formal salvage sector cannot process quickly enough. In an infrastructure crisis, the copper stripper works longer hours because more cable is being replaced. The system that most needs to be understood at times of economic stress is the system that conventional economic analysis is least equipped to see.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Route Holders
Katlehong / Vosloorus Residential Grid

Across the residential streets of Katlehong and Vosloorus, established scrap collectors maintain defined territorial routes with a consistency that amounts to a private property right — informal, unwritten, but enforced by the social norms of the buying points they sell to, which will not knowingly buy material taken from another collector's territory. A route holder who has worked the same twelve streets for seven years carries knowledge that cannot be replicated by a newcomer in less than a year: the household that generates cable offcuts from its informal electrical work, the spaza shop owner who accumulates aluminium tins in the back room, the building that is being quietly renovated and will produce steel window frames and copper pipe in the coming weeks. This knowledge is the route's capital. The route is the business. Its value, if it could be formally recognised, would be considerable.

2. The Non-Ferrous Strippers
Brakpan Light Industrial Zone

In a cluster of modest workshops along the Brakpan industrial strip, a group of specialist operators has built businesses around a single technical competence: the extraction of copper and aluminium from mixed electrical and mechanical scrap at a purity level that commands a significant premium over unsorted non-ferrous material. They strip insulation from cable by hand or with small mechanical strippers, sort conductors by alloy grade, and accumulate cleaned material until they have enough to negotiate directly with the large non-ferrous processors rather than selling through an intermediary. The price differential between clean stripped copper and mixed cable scrap is large enough to make the stripping operation genuinely profitable at small scale, and the technical knowledge — knowing which cable grades contain which alloy, understanding the tolerance requirements of the processors who will buy — is accumulated through practice rather than through any formal training. These operators are, in effect, running small-scale metallurgical processing operations from corrugated iron sheds.

3. The Mobile Buyers
Germiston / Boksburg Industrial Fringe

Operating from bakkies fitted with platform scales and stocked with cash, a network of mobile buying agents moves through Ekurhuleni's industrial zones offering same-day collection of industrial scrap — offcuts, failed castings, swarf, decommissioned tooling — from factories and workshops that generate material irregularly and in quantities too small to attract a scheduled collection from a large processor. The mobile buyer's advantage is his flexibility: he can turn up within an hour of a call, weigh on-site, and pay cash. His margin comes from aggregating small loads and delivering consolidated material to a mid-tier aggregator at a spread that reflects his transport costs and the value he has added by providing on-demand collection. These operators know the production cycles of their regular clients with some precision — knowing, for instance, that a particular press shop generates significant steel offcuts on the last Thursday of each month when it switches between product runs — and time their circuits accordingly.

4. The Map of the Week
The Cleveland Road Scrap Corridor, Germiston–Boksburg
26°12′S  28°11′E  →  26°12′S  28°14′E

Approximately three kilometres. That is the active length of the scrap dealer and processor corridor running along the Cleveland Road axis between central Germiston and the Boksburg industrial fringe — a stretch that contains, within a single road corridor, more secondary metal processing capacity than most South African provinces can claim in total. In those three kilometres you will find: the large aggregator yards with their baling presses and overhead cranes; the specialist processors handling specific commodity streams — stainless, copper, aluminium — in enclosed facilities with overhead extraction; the smaller buying points that serve as the interface between the mobile collectors and the larger processor tier; and the weighbridge operators whose transaction records, if they could be aggregated, would constitute the most accurate account of secondary metal flows in the East Rand that exists anywhere.

What you will not find is any public documentation of what the corridor does or how much it processes. No industry body claims it. No municipal economic profile describes it. The trucks that move through it carry no manifest that any planning department has thought to count. To stand at the corridor's busiest weighbridge at nine in the morning, watching tipper after tipper queue to discharge its load, is to understand that Ekurhuleni has a commodity exchange. It simply does not know it yet.

5. The East Rand Number
~2 million t Estimated annual volume of ferrous and non-ferrous scrap recycled through South Africa's formal and informal metal recovery industry

South Africa's scrap metal recovery industry — formal and informal combined — processes an estimated two million tonnes of ferrous and non-ferrous metal annually, supplying roughly 30% of the domestic steel industry's raw material requirements as secondary feedstock. The East Rand, as the country's most concentrated zone of legacy heavy industry, mining infrastructure, and dense township settlement, generates a disproportionate share of this material. The collectors, buying points, and aggregators who handle it constitute a supply chain that touches global commodity markets without being named, counted, or credited. Secondary steel has a carbon footprint approximately 75% lower than primary steel production. The man with the trolley is, among other things, an environmental services provider. The invoice for that service has never been sent.

6. Closing Reflection
The Weight of What Is Overlooked

There is a useful thought experiment for understanding the scrap economy's actual importance: imagine, for one month, that the collectors stopped collecting. The buying points would empty within days. The aggregator yards would run out of feedstock within a week. The mills that depend on East Rand scrap as a proportion of their melt would begin to feel the gap within a fortnight. The export containers would ship light. The cascade of consequences from a month-long interruption to an activity that most economic analyses do not acknowledge would be, in its way, clarifying. The things that are truly essential tend to make themselves known by their absence. The scrap economy of the East Rand has not yet had occasion to make itself known in this way. It simply continues, morning after morning, trolley by trolley, bale by bale — moving the metal that the city no longer needs into the hands of the industries that need it most, at a price the market sets, along a route that nobody planned and nobody could now do without.
