The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 9

The Alberton-Germiston Manufacturing Ridge

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Alberton-Germiston Manufacturing Ridge is not a story about factories. It is a story about what it means for a city to keep making things when the rest of the country decided that making things was no longer the point. While South Africa's economic narrative over the past three decades has been dominated by finance, retail, and telecommunications, a dense corridor of heavy manufacturing has continued to operate along the ridge between Alberton and Germiston — largely undiscussed and thoroughly underestimated.

The deep story: how the Alberton-Germiston corridor has retained a manufacturing density unlike almost anywhere else in inland South Africa — producing cables, chemicals, steel components, automotive parts, and industrial equipment for markets stretching from Cape Town to Lusaka — and why this persistence, far from being an anachronism, represents an economic foundation that the service economy cannot replicate.

Three supporting micro-stories: the high-voltage cable manufacturers of Germiston who quietly supply the infrastructure projects of half the continent; the die-casting and toolmaking operations of Alberton's southern industrial zone that feed the automotive aftermarket from a base most people drive past without registering; and the informal chemical blenders of the Cleveland-Germiston interface who have built a grey-market supply chain servicing industrial customers the formal sector prices out.

The overlooked place: New Era Road in Germiston — a two-kilometre strip of active heavy manufacturing running parallel to the mainline railway, where the smell of hot metal and cutting fluid announces an industrial world that most Johannesburg commuters did not know still existed.

The interesting statistic: greater Germiston is home to over 3,000 registered manufacturing operations — making it, by some estimates, the most densely packed manufacturing zone per square kilometre in sub-Saharan Africa.

Weekly Issue — 20 March 2026

The Ridge That Never Stopped Making Things

How the Alberton-Germiston corridor quietly outlasted South Africa's pivot to services — and why that stubbornness may be its most undervalued economic asset.

1. Editorial
The Smoke That Stayed

There is a moment, driving west on the N3 from Germiston toward the Johannesburg city centre, when the skyline does something unexpected. The office towers of Sandton are thirty kilometres away, invisible and largely irrelevant. What you see instead, on both sides of the highway, is a low, dense landscape of factory roofs, cooling stacks, and electricity pylons — the physical grammar of a manufacturing city that has declined to be replaced. The smoke from the metalworking plants mixes with the morning air. A copper wire-drawing facility is running its third shift. In a corrugated-iron shed that is larger than it looks from the road, someone is pouring molten aluminium into a die.

I have spent time in enough South African cities to know how unusual this is. The standard trajectory for industrial zones of major urban centres has been retreat: factories converted to warehouses, warehouses converted to lifestyle developments, the residual smell of industry gradually replaced by coffee and artisanal bread. Germiston, and its quieter neighbour Alberton to the south, have largely refused this script. The factories are still there. In many cases, they are still growing.

This is not simply a story about industrial heritage or a sentimental attachment to the idea of making things. It is a story about competitive geography. The Alberton-Germiston corridor sits at the precise intersection of the Gauteng rail network, the N3 and N17 highway arteries, and the logistics ecosystem generated by OR Tambo International Airport. It has infrastructure connectivity and a labour catchment that spans Katlehong, Vosloorus, Alberton South, and Germiston itself. These are not romantic advantages. They are the kind of structural advantages that make industrial economies work decade after decade, regardless of what the broader economic narrative is doing.

What has surprised me, in looking at this corridor carefully, is not that manufacturing persisted here. It is that the manufacturing which persisted is considerably more sophisticated than the region's industrial reputation suggests. This is not a cluster of declining legacy plants running on old machinery and borrowed time. It is, in significant part, a living industrial system — one that has adapted, disaggregated, and quietly upgraded while the story of South African manufacturing was being written elsewhere, mostly in the register of failure.

This issue is an attempt to describe what that system actually looks like from the inside.

2. The Long Essay
The Quiet Industrial Persistence of the Germiston-Alberton Ridge
Section I — The Observation

Drive the length of New Era Road in Germiston on a Tuesday morning — not at commuter pace, but slowly, the way you might walk a market — and you will encounter, in roughly two kilometres, a wire and cable manufacturer supplying sub-transmission projects in East Africa, a chemical blending operation whose tanker trucks are already disappearing toward the N12 before dawn, a pressed-steel components plant running automotive door hardware for an OEM whose name you would recognise, and a series of smaller operations — toolmakers, die-casters, heat-treaters — whose frontages offer no indication of the technical complexity operating behind them.

The corridor is not photogenic. The aesthetic is corrugated iron, painted concrete, and the particular grime that comes from metal being worked rather than stored. There are no glass facades. There are no corporate logos large enough to read from the road. What there is, unmistakably, is production — the sounds and smells of materials being transformed, shifted, assembled, drawn into shape. It is the antithesis of the logistics park, which is all about moving things. This is about changing them.

A manufacturing cluster is not just an economic unit. It is a form of accumulated memory — and this one has been remembering for over a century.
Section II — The Hidden System

The manufacturing system of the Alberton-Germiston corridor operates on three tiers that are rarely discussed together. The first is the tier most people know, or think they know: the large, named manufacturers — the cable plants, the AECI and Dow chemical operations, the automotive component suppliers feeding the Ekurhuleni supply chain. These are visible, documented, and occasionally reported on. They are the capstones of the system.

The second tier is the mid-size industrial ecosystem that has grown in their shadow: the component suppliers, specialist fabricators, and precision engineering operations that make parts for the automotive, mining, and energy sectors. These companies are not household names. They employ, typically, between fifty and three hundred people. They operate in the industrial parks of Germiston East, Alberton South, and the Cleveland interface zone. Their customers are other manufacturers, not consumers. Their existence is therefore invisible to anyone who does not share their supply chain.

The third tier is where the system becomes genuinely interesting: the informal and semi-formal manufacturers who have built operations in the margins of the formal cluster. Ex-employees of formal plants who have set up their own toolmaking workshops. Die-casting operations running on reclaimed equipment. Small-batch chemical producers blending products for customers the formal chemical companies find uneconomical to serve. This third tier does not appear in any industrial survey. It does not contribute to the official manufacturing output statistics. But it performs functions — rapid prototyping, short-run fabrication, bespoke chemical formulation — that the formal tier either cannot or will not offer at prices its downstream customers can afford. It is, in effect, the R&D and flexibility layer for the industrial cluster as a whole.

Section III — The Broader Insight

The persistence of the Alberton-Germiston manufacturing ridge tells a story that South Africa's development policy has been slow to absorb. The dominant economic narrative of the past three decades — that manufacturing was an industrial-era activity being superseded by services and the knowledge economy — turns out to be significantly more complicated than it appeared. Manufacturing matters for reasons that service economies cannot easily replicate. It creates embedded technical knowledge. It builds supply chain relationships that are hard to relocate. And it provides employment at middle-skill levels that the service economy, which tends toward the high-skill and low-skill with little in between, does not reliably generate.

The Germiston-Alberton corridor is one of the few places in South Africa where this anchoring effect is still clearly legible. The density of industrial knowledge in this relatively small geography — the engineers who know how to wind a high-voltage cable, the metallurgists who understand the properties of a specific aluminium alloy, the chemical technicians who can formulate a bespoke industrial cleaner from first principles — represents an accumulation of human capital that took generations to build and that could not be rebuilt quickly if it dispersed. Once a manufacturing cluster loses its embedded knowledge, it does not come back. The buildings remain. The knowledge does not.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The manufacturing system of the Alberton-Germiston corridor has been battered by the same forces that have damaged South African manufacturing broadly: the electricity crisis, the skills exodus, competition from Asian imports, infrastructure deterioration. And yet it persists. More than persists: it has, in certain sectors, become more adaptive under pressure.

The mechanism is the same one visible in the airport economy and the Springs corridor: when the formal system contracts, the informal and mid-tier operations absorb the released capacity, workforce, and supply relationships. When a large cable manufacturer narrows its product range to focus on high-volume lines, the gap in specialised, low-volume products is filled by a smaller operation running ex-capacity equipment purchased at a fraction of replacement cost. When a formal chemical plant closes a product line, the formulation knowledge walks out the door with the departing process engineer and reappears, within months, in a smaller facility three suburbs away.

This is antifragility in its most industrial form. The formal system is efficient but brittle. The informal system is slower and less polished but far more adaptable. The corridor's resilience comes not from its largest plants but from the dense network of smaller operations that have learned to profit from the gaps the large ones leave behind. Shock after shock, the ridge absorbs, reconfigures, and continues to produce.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Cable Winders
Germiston East Industrial Zone

In the back half of Germiston's eastern industrial strip, a network of small and mid-scale cable manufacturing operations has carved out a sustainable niche by doing what the large manufacturers cannot: running short production batches at competitive prices. The large operators, geared for volume, find it uneconomical to retool for specialty orders of a few hundred metres. The smaller operations have built their entire model around precisely these orders. Their customers include rural electrification contractors, mining operations requiring non-standard specifications, and the informal electrical contractors who wire much of Ekurhuleni's township housing stock. They do not advertise. They operate on referral and reputation. Their order books are consistently full.

2. The Alberton Die-Casters
Alberton South Industrial

Along the industrial perimeter of Alberton South, a cluster of aluminium and zinc die-casting operations has quietly built a regional supply position in the automotive aftermarket. They produce — under no brand name, for no named client — the brackets, housings, and structural components sold through the automotive parts distribution networks of Boksburg and Germiston. The formal automotive supply chain does not acknowledge them. The automotive parts market could not function without them. Their competitive advantage is speed and flexibility: they can tool and produce a first-run component in days rather than weeks, at a unit cost the formal tier cannot approach on short runs.

3. The Cleveland Chemical Blenders
Cleveland / Germiston Interface

At the western edge of the manufacturing ridge, where Germiston meets the Cleveland industrial zone, a network of small chemical blending operations produces industrial cleaning agents, surface treatment chemicals, and specialty lubricants for the manufacturing sector. They use base chemicals sourced from the large formal distributors nearby, combining them into formulations the majors either do not offer or price beyond the reach of small manufacturers. Their customers are the workshops, fabricators, and small-batch producers of the ridge itself — a supply chain that is, in a meaningful sense, self-sustaining: the ridge feeds its own inputs.

4. The Map of the Week
New Era Road, Germiston
26°12′S 28°10′E

Two kilometres. That is the working length of New Era Road as it runs north through Germiston's core industrial zone, parallel to the mainline railway that has been the physical spine of Ekurhuleni's industrial economy for more than a century. In those two kilometres, the density of active manufacturing operations per street frontage is extraordinary by any count. Cable plants. Chemical operations. Pressed-steel fabricators. Toolmakers. Heat-treatment facilities. Industrial gas suppliers. The road carries the constant traffic of materials arriving and finished goods departing — a rhythm that has barely changed in fifty years.

What is notable is not only that these operations exist, but that they are visibly working. New Era Road is not an industrial heritage site. It is a functioning industrial artery. To drive it slowly, at production pace rather than commuter pace, is to understand that Ekurhuleni did not once become an industrial city and then stop. It still is one — and this two-kilometre corridor is as clear a proof as exists anywhere in inland South Africa.

5. The East Rand Number
3,000+ Registered manufacturing operations in the greater Germiston industrial zone

Greater Germiston contains, by the most recent available estimates, upwards of three thousand registered manufacturing operations — a density that has few comparisons in inland sub-Saharan Africa. This figure excludes the informal and semi-formal operations that add an unknown additional layer of productive capacity. It includes operations ranging from multi-hundred-employee cable and chemical plants to single-shed toolmakers employing four people. Together, they represent a concentration of industrial knowledge, supply chain infrastructure, and embedded manufacturing skill that took the better part of a century to accumulate — and that cannot be rebuilt, if it is lost, on any short-term timescale the policy imagination tends to work within.

6. Closing Reflection
What the Ridge Remembers

There is something worth holding onto in the persistence of the Alberton-Germiston manufacturing ridge. In a country that has spent a generation being told that the future belongs to services and the knowledge economy, this corridor has simply continued to make things — cables, chemicals, castings, components — and in doing so has preserved a form of economic capacity that no amount of fintech or platform commerce can substitute. The factories on New Era Road are not waiting for a policy intervention to justify their existence. They have already made the case, in quarterly production runs, in export invoices, in the wages of three shifts of workers. The question is whether the institutions nominally responsible for economic development will eventually catch up with what the ridge has been quietly proving for years: that the economy that makes things is not the past. It is the foundation on which everything else is built.
